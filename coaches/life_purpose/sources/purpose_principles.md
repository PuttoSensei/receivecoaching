# Life Purpose Principles

## Purpose is an orientation, not a destination

A purpose is the direction you face, not a place you arrive at. You can fulfill a purpose for sixty years and never be done with it. If what you are looking for is a thing you can check off a list, you are looking for an achievement, not a purpose.

## The four questions (a practical frame)

1. **What do I love?** What do I lose track of time doing? What was I drawn to as a child before I learned to be strategic?
2. **What am I good at?** What do people ask me for help with? Where does competence come easily?
3. **What does the world need?** What problem do I see that most people walk past? What would I want to exist in twenty years that doesn't yet?
4. **What can sustain me?** What lets me keep the lights on while I do this?

The overlap of the first three is meaning. The overlap of all four is vocation. You usually need at least two of them active at any given life stage.

## Purpose is plural and layered

The myth: there is one true calling and your job is to find it. The reality: most people have three or four layered purposes — as a parent, as a maker, as a friend, as a citizen — and the mix shifts by decade. A thirty-year-old's central purpose is usually not a sixty-year-old's.

If you are searching for a single grand purpose and feeling lost, try asking: what are the three or four purposes I am already living, whether I named them or not?

## Watch where your energy goes

What you actually spend time on — when no one is watching, when no one is paying you — tells you more about your purpose than any self-assessment. If you say your purpose is X but you consistently drift toward Y, Y is worth investigating.

## The deathbed test

Imagine yourself on your deathbed. Not morbidly — honestly. What would you regret not having done? Not having tried? Not having said? Whatever comes up first, unfiltered, is signal. The things that feel urgent in the moment rarely appear on this list. The things that feel indulgent or selfish often do.

## Ordinary purpose

A good life does not require a world-changing mission. Raising children who are kinder than you were. Making one honest thing. Being a true friend to five people across four decades. Showing up for a local community. These are not consolation prizes — they are complete lives. The pressure to have an extraordinary purpose is often cultural noise.

## Purpose as hiding place

Sometimes \"searching for purpose\" is a way to avoid a concrete problem — a marriage that needs attention, a job that needs to end, money that needs managing, a body that needs care. If you find yourself reaching for purpose-talk when a practical thing is unresolved, handle the practical thing first. Clarity often comes once the fog of unaddressed life clears.

## Start small

You do not need to change your life before you live your purpose. You can start with an hour a week. Write the book one page at a time. Teach one student. Plant one garden. Purpose at scale is built out of purpose at a small scale that you kept doing.
