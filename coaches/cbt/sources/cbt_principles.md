# CBT-Style Coaching: Tools & Principles

## Important note on scope

Cognitive Behavioural Therapy is a clinical treatment with strong evidence for depression, anxiety disorders, PTSD, OCD, and more — delivered by trained therapists. A "CBT coach" is not a CBT therapist. The tools below are useful for everyday stuck thinking; they are not a substitute for clinical treatment when clinical treatment is needed. If what you're dealing with feels bigger than this, see a qualified therapist.

## The core model

A situation does not cause a feeling directly. A situation triggers a thought, and the thought drives the feeling and the behaviour.

**Situation → Thought → Emotion → Behaviour**

- Your partner doesn't reply for three hours (situation).
- *"They don't care about me"* (thought).
- Hurt, anxious (emotion).
- You send a passive-aggressive message or withdraw (behaviour).

Change the thought, the emotion and behaviour shift. The situation is often unchangeable in the moment. The thought usually is.

## Automatic thoughts

The thoughts that drive most of your feelings are automatic — fast, habitual, unexamined. They often aren't even formed as sentences until you catch them and write them down. The first skill in CBT-style work is learning to notice them.

When you catch yourself feeling something strongly, stop and ask: *what just went through my mind?* Write it down as closely to the actual wording as you can. That wording is the data.

## The cognitive distortions

Specific patterns of thinking that systematically distort reality. Most unhelpful automatic thoughts fit one or more:

- **All-or-nothing:** "If I'm not perfect, I'm a failure." No middle.
- **Catastrophising:** "If this presentation goes badly, my career is over."
- **Overgeneralisation:** "She didn't reply. Nobody ever wants to hear from me."
- **Mind-reading:** "He's definitely thinking I'm incompetent."
- **Fortune-telling:** "I already know this is going to go wrong."
- **Emotional reasoning:** "I feel guilty, so I must have done something wrong."
- **Should statements:** "I should be further along by now." (Shoulds produce shame, not change.)
- **Personalisation:** "The team meeting was tense. It must be because of me."
- **Labelling:** Not "I failed at this" but "I am a failure."
- **Discounting the positive:** "Sure, they thanked me, but they were just being polite."
- **Mental filter:** Fifty compliments and one criticism — you dwell on the one.

Naming the distortion is half the work. Once you can say "ah, that's catastrophising" the thought loses some of its grip.

## The thought record

The foundational tool. Seven columns, filled in honestly:

1. **Situation** — what happened, factually. Who, what, when, where.
2. **Automatic thought** — the exact thought, in your own words.
3. **Emotion** — named and rated 0–100.
4. **Evidence that supports the thought** — what makes it feel true?
5. **Evidence against the thought** — what doesn't fit?
6. **Balanced alternative** — a more accurate thought, not a cheerful one.
7. **Re-rated emotion** — 0–100 after the work.

The balanced alternative is the output. It isn't "I'll ace this." It's "This is a hard presentation. I've handled hard presentations before. It probably won't go perfectly, and that's okay."

Do this on paper, not in your head. The act of writing is doing most of the work.

## Three useful questions

When you don't have time for a full thought record, three questions go a long way:

1. **What's the evidence?** Not how I feel — what evidence would hold up in court?
2. **What would I say to a friend thinking this?** We are much kinder and clearer with friends than ourselves.
3. **If the worst happened, could I handle it?** Catastrophic thoughts shrink when you engage with the feared outcome directly rather than flinching away.

## Behavioural activation

Sometimes cognitive work alone is weak. When you are low, avoidant, or ruminating, thinking differently is hard — but acting differently is possible, and the thoughts often follow.

The principle: action first, mood follows. Waiting to feel like doing things is how people stay stuck for months. Schedule the thing. Do the thing. Often the feeling arrives during or after.

Good candidates:
- **Pleasant activities** — a walk, calling a friend, being outside, making food.
- **Mastery activities** — one small completed task. Folded laundry counts.
- **Social activities** — contact with one person you trust.

Track mood 0–10 before and after. Notice the reliable pattern: mood almost always improves. The brain needs this evidence repeatedly before it stops believing "I can't do anything."

## Graded exposure / avoidance breaking

If you are avoiding something — a hard conversation, a fear, a task — avoidance reinforces the anxiety. Each avoidance makes the next one more likely.

The fix is graded: not one big confrontation, but a ladder of small exposures.

- If the thing is sending a difficult email: first write it without sending. Then read it out loud. Then send a simpler version. Then the real one.
- If the thing is going back to the gym: first put gym clothes on. Then drive there and come home. Then go for 10 minutes. Then normally.

Each rung normalises the next. The anxiety drops with practice, not with thought.

## Shoulds are shame-producers

A "should" without a plan is a shame machine. "I should be exercising more" does nothing except make you feel bad. "I will walk 20 minutes on Monday, Wednesday, Friday at 7 am" is doing something.

Audit your shoulds. Either convert each to a scheduled action or drop it entirely. Keep the ones you're actually going to act on. Let the rest go.

## The goal is accuracy, not positivity

CBT is sometimes mistaken for "think positive". It isn't. The goal is to think *accurately*. If your thought is distorted negatively, you move toward accuracy (which will feel more positive). If your thought is unrealistically positive (denial, magical thinking), you move toward accuracy (which may feel less cheerful).

Accurate thought leads to effective action. Positive thought alone often doesn't.
