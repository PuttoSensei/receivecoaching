# Ketogenic Diet Principles (Education Only)

## Critical safety note

A ketogenic diet is a significant metabolic intervention and is NOT appropriate for everyone. Before starting, check with a doctor — especially if you have diabetes (Type 1 especially, but also Type 2 on medication), kidney disease, a history of pancreatitis or gallbladder issues, heart disease or familial hypercholesterolemia, thyroid conditions, an eating disorder history, are pregnant or breastfeeding, or take any medication that may require dose adjustment.

If you have ANY of the above: talk to your doctor first. This is not optional.

This document is general education, not medical advice.

## What ketosis is

When dietary carbohydrate is low enough for long enough, the body depletes its stored glycogen and shifts its primary fuel to fat — both dietary fat and body fat. As part of this, the liver produces ketone bodies (beta-hydroxybutyrate, acetoacetate, acetone) that fuel tissues including the brain.

This is a normal metabolic state, evolutionarily ancient — the body enters mild ketosis overnight and during fasting. A ketogenic diet simply maintains it continuously.

Nutritional ketosis (what the diet aims for) is different from diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA), a medical emergency in Type 1 diabetes where ketones accumulate dangerously alongside high blood glucose. Non-diabetics and well-controlled Type 2 diabetics do not develop DKA from a ketogenic diet. Type 1 diabetics should not attempt ketogenic protocols without close medical supervision.

## The macro ratios

A well-formulated ketogenic diet typically runs at:

- **Carbohydrates:** ~5–10% of calories, or 20–50g net carbs per day. ("Net carbs" = total carbs minus fibre.)
- **Protein:** 15–25% of calories. Typically 0.8–1.2g per pound of lean body mass.
- **Fat:** the remainder, usually 70–80% of calories.

Note: protein is moderate, not low. The old "high-fat low-protein" framing was wrong. Too little protein causes muscle loss; too much is fine for most people and does not reliably kick you out of ketosis.

## Foods that fit

**Eat freely:** non-starchy vegetables (leafy greens, broccoli, cauliflower, zucchini, peppers, cabbage, asparagus), eggs, fish, meat, poultry, nuts and seeds in moderation, full-fat dairy if tolerated (cheese, Greek yogurt, cream), avocado, olives, olive oil, avocado oil, butter, coconut oil.

**Small amounts:** berries, nuts (watch portion), some dairy, tomato, onion.

**Avoid or minimise:** grains (bread, pasta, rice, oats, cereal), sugar and sugary foods, most fruit, legumes (beans, lentils, chickpeas), starchy vegetables (potato, sweet potato, corn, peas), most processed foods, sugary drinks, beer.

## The transition: "keto flu"

The first 3–7 days are often uncomfortable. Fatigue, headache, irritability, brain fog, cramps, poor sleep, dizziness. This is not the diet failing. It is your body shifting fuel systems and, crucially, losing electrolytes.

When insulin drops, the kidneys excrete sodium, and water goes with it. Potassium and magnesium follow. This, more than anything else, causes the transition symptoms.

**The fix is electrolytes, from day 1:**
- **Sodium:** add salt to meals liberally. 3–5g total daily is common (this is more than most low-sodium guidelines, which assume a high-carb diet). Broth, salted water, or an electrolyte mix.
- **Potassium:** from foods (leafy greens, avocado, salmon, nuts) and sometimes low-sodium salt or a supplement.
- **Magnesium:** from foods and usually a supplement (200–400mg/day of magnesium glycinate or citrate).

Most people who "can't do keto" gave up because they under-ate electrolytes. Electrolytes from day one make the transition far easier.

## Fat adaptation

Reaching ketosis takes 3–7 days. Becoming *fat-adapted* — where the body runs efficiently on fat and ketones including during exercise — takes 3–6 weeks.

During this window, exercise performance often dips, then recovers. Endurance athletes usually do well once adapted. High-intensity and explosive performance can be more mixed; some people use targeted carbs around training.

## Common pitfalls

- **Hidden carbs.** Sauces, dressings, processed meats, supposedly "keto" products. Read labels.
- **Under-eating protein.** Keeps you hungry and loses muscle. Aim for target protein first, then fill with fat to satiety.
- **Over-eating fat when weight loss is the goal.** Fat is calorically dense. If you're not losing weight and that's your goal, fat likely needs to come down — body fat is what you're trying to burn.
- **Constipation.** Low fibre is common. Increase non-starchy vegetables, chia seeds, and water.
- **Social friction.** Eating with others, restaurants, family events become harder. Plan ahead.
- **All-or-nothing thinking.** Missing ketosis for a day is not the end. Just resume.

## Measuring ketosis (if you want to)

- **Urine ketone strips:** cheap, only accurate in the first few weeks (once fat-adapted, strips show less even when ketosis is present).
- **Blood ketone meter:** the gold standard. Measures beta-hydroxybutyrate. 0.5–3.0 mmol/L is nutritional ketosis.
- **Breath ketone meters:** reusable, one-time cost, less precise than blood but useful.

Honestly: most people don't need to measure. If you are under 50g net carbs, sticking to the food list, and not eating sugar or starch, you are almost certainly in ketosis after a few days.

## Sustainability and exit

The best keto diet is one you can actually maintain, or that you transition off cleanly.

Common approaches:
- **Strict ongoing keto** — some people genuinely do better here long-term.
- **Cyclical keto** — weekdays keto, weekends flexible.
- **Targeted keto** — carbs around exercise.
- **Time-limited keto** — a block of 3–6 months to reset metabolism, then a Mediterranean or whole-food style diet.

When exiting keto, do it gradually — reintroducing carbs slowly over a week avoids a big rebound in water weight and digestive upset. Expect to regain some "water weight" regardless; this is not fat.

## What keto is good for (for those medically cleared)

Evidence is strongest for:
- Weight loss, for some people.
- Blood sugar regulation in Type 2 diabetes (with medical supervision for medication adjustment).
- Epilepsy (a well-established therapeutic indication, under medical supervision).
- Some cases of metabolic syndrome, PCOS, fatty liver.

Evidence is mixed or limited for: performance enhancement, neurodegenerative conditions, cancer adjunct therapy (experimental), mood disorders.

Evidence does not support: as a universal optimal diet for everyone. Many people thrive on other eating patterns.

## When to stop or pause

If you experience: worsening cholesterol beyond reasonable metabolic changes, persistent fatigue beyond the initial transition, hair loss, menstrual disruption, sleep disturbance, intense food preoccupation, or any symptoms that concern you — stop and see a doctor.

Keto works well for some. It doesn't work for everyone. Respect your body's feedback.

## If disordered eating is in the picture

If you have a history of restrictive eating, binge-restrict cycles, or obsessive food thoughts, keto is usually the wrong tool. The restriction structure can activate disordered patterns. Seek specialised support instead.
