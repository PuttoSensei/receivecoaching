# ADHD Principles

## Scope note

This is coaching guidance, not clinical treatment. For diagnosis and medication, see a qualified clinician. Medication is the single most effective treatment for most people with ADHD; coaching supports it, it does not replace it.

## It's not laziness

The single most important frame. ADHD is a neurodevelopmental difference in executive function, dopamine regulation, and attention control. The brain is wired differently.

Standard productivity advice is designed for neurotypical brains. It often fails for ADHD brains — and the failure is not a moral one. It is a mismatch between tool and brain.

"Just try harder" has never worked for ADHD. Never will. Trying harder is often exactly what the person has been doing, unsuccessfully, for decades.

## The actual challenges

ADHD affects several executive function domains:

- **Task initiation.** The gap between knowing you should and actually starting. Often enormous.
- **Sustained attention.** Can you stay on the task once started? Or does the mind pull elsewhere?
- **Working memory.** Holding information in mind while using it. Often weak — which is why ADHD brains forget what they walked into the room for.
- **Time perception.** Time blindness. "Now" and "not now" are the two main time categories. Next Tuesday doesn't feel real.
- **Emotional regulation.** Emotions come fast and strong. RSD (rejection sensitive dysphoria) amplifies perceived criticism.
- **Hyperfocus.** The paradox: intense focus on something engaging, often at the cost of everything else.

The coaching works with each of these specifically.

## Externalise everything

ADHD working memory is unreliable. Things held only in the head WILL be forgotten. Not might. Will.

- **Calendar.** Every appointment, deadline, commitment. If it's not in the calendar, it doesn't exist.
- **Task capture system.** Any one that you'll use. Notebook, app, sticky notes. The specific tool matters less than consistent capture.
- **Visible reminders.** Physical objects where you'll see them. The thing you need to remember goes on top of the thing you'll definitely use.
- **Lists over memory.** Always.
- **Default timers.** For everything. Cooking, work blocks, transitions.

The rule: what's in your head will be lost. What's external and visible will be seen.

## Dopamine-friendly work design

ADHD brains are low-dopamine. Work that's interesting, novel, urgent, or challenging produces dopamine and is easier. Work that's boring, repetitive, or distant doesn't.

Design for this:
- **Make boring tasks more stimulating.** Music, body doubling, changing location, a reward afterward.
- **Use novelty.** Rotate tasks more than neurotypical people do.
- **Leverage urgency.** Short deadlines work better than long ones. Deadline in 2 days often produces results; deadline in 2 months produces nothing until day 58.
- **Challenge mode.** Turn tasks into mini-games or time challenges.
- **Batch dull tasks** into single blocks so the rest of the day can be interesting.

Working with the dopamine system is far more effective than willpower.

## Body doubling

Working alongside another person dramatically increases follow-through for ADHD brains. In-person, video call, or virtual body-doubling rooms.

Why: external attention regulates internal attention. The presence of another focused person cues your own brain to focus.

This is not a quirk. It is a research-supported intervention. Use it.

## Time externalised

ADHD brains don't feel time passing. "I'll just check this for a minute" becomes 90 minutes. "I have plenty of time before the meeting" becomes running in five minutes late.

Tools:
- **Visual timers** (Time Timer is a brand designed for this). Seeing time pass as a shrinking coloured segment regulates the perception.
- **Clocks in every room.** Visible clocks, not digital-phone-buried ones.
- **Set alarms for transitions**, not just for arrival. "Alarm to start getting ready to leave" is different from "alarm to leave."
- **Double-check travel and prep time.** ADHD planning systematically underestimates it.

## Task initiation hacks

Starting is usually the hardest part. Once started, ADHD brains often do fine. So: make starting easier.

- **2-minute commitment.** "I'll just do 2 minutes of this." Often turns into 30.
- **The smallest possible first action.** Not "clean the kitchen" — "pick up one dish."
- **Implementation intentions.** "At 10am on Tuesday, immediately after my coffee, I will sit at my desk and open the document."
- **Habit stacking.** Attach new tasks to existing routines (after coffee, after brushing teeth).
- **External accountability.** Tell someone. Schedule it. Put it in front of someone who'll notice.

## Hyperfocus management

When it's working, you can do 8 hours of work in 3. When it's not, you stare at the screen for hours doing nothing.

Hyperfocus isn't a superpower you can summon — it's a state that arrives when the work is interesting enough. The goal isn't to produce it on demand. It's to:
- Ride it when it comes.
- Protect yourself from its costs (skipped meals, missed meetings, sleep lost, other important things neglected).
- Set alarms for breaks, water, food, meetings you cannot miss.
- Accept that after hyperfocus comes a crash. Plan for it.

## RSD (rejection sensitive dysphoria)

One of the least-discussed ADHD features. Perceived criticism, rejection, or disapproval produces an emotional response disproportionate to the event. Feels like physical pain. Can drive avoidance, perfectionism, people-pleasing, or rage.

It is neurobiological, not a character flaw. Naming it helps. Knowing it will pass helps. Pre-deciding how you'll respond to criticism helps. If severe, medication (stimulant or non-stimulant) often reduces it; therapy helps with the patterns that built around it.

## Environment design

For ADHD brains especially, the environment does half the work.

- **Out of sight = out of mind.** What you want to do must be visible. What you need to avoid must be inaccessible.
- **Clutter is attention tax.** Visually loud environments drain focus. Minimise what's visible when working.
- **One screen, one tab when possible.** Context switching is expensive for ADHD brains.
- **Predictable setup.** The same chair, same desk, same playlist can become a focus cue.

## What doesn't work for ADHD

- **Complex productivity systems** (GTD, Bullet Journal, elaborate task apps). Most ADHDers have tried and abandoned several. The overhead of maintaining them exceeds the benefit. Simple works.
- **"Discipline yourself."** The lever that's broken in ADHD is precisely the discipline lever. Needing to discipline yourself to use a system defeats the purpose.
- **Shame-based motivation.** Works short-term, devastating long-term. The ADHD brain builds avoidance around anything attached to shame.
- **Neurotypical productivity advice.** "Do your hardest task first thing in the morning" assumes a neurotypical morning dopamine curve. For ADHD, the best block is often later and depends on medication timing.

## Medication (brief, respectful note)

Medication (stimulant or non-stimulant) is the single most effective treatment for most people with ADHD. The research is robust. Coaching + medication outperforms either alone for most people.

Resistance to medication is common and often comes from cultural noise, not evidence. A personal, honest conversation with a qualified psychiatrist is worth having if you haven't already.

This is not medical advice. It is a suggestion to explore an option with a qualified professional.

## Common co-occurring

ADHD often comes with:
- Anxiety
- Depression
- Learning differences (dyslexia, dyscalculia)
- Autism (AuDHD)
- Sleep disorders
- Addiction vulnerabilities

If any of these are present alongside ADHD, address them alongside. They compound.

## Strengths to claim

ADHD is not just deficits. Traits that often come with it:
- Creativity and lateral thinking
- Pattern recognition
- High energy and enthusiasm
- Hyperfocus on what matters to you
- Crisis performance (you're good in emergencies)
- Empathy and attunement to others
- Curiosity across many domains
- Willingness to try things
- Entrepreneurial and divergent thinking

Many successful creatives, founders, and leaders have ADHD. The trait can work with you, not just against you, when the life is designed around it.

## A weekly rhythm

- **One external capture system.** Use it all week.
- **Calendar everything.** Trust nothing to memory.
- **Visible timers** during work blocks.
- **Body double** for at least one hard task.
- **One environment shift** if stuck.
- **Sleep, food, water, movement.** The basics are load-bearing for ADHD brains. They fall apart fastest when basics are neglected.
- **Self-compassion.** The brain is different. Shaming it doesn't change it. Working with it does.
