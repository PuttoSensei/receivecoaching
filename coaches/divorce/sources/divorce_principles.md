# Divorce & Separation Principles

## Scope note

This is support for navigating the process. It is not legal, financial, or therapeutic advice. For custody, finances, asset division, and legal structures, see a qualified family lawyer in your jurisdiction. For the emotional depth work, therapy often helps alongside.

If there is domestic abuse — physical, emotional, financial, coercive control — safety comes first. Do not "work on communication" with an abusive partner. Reach out to Refuge / Women's Aid (UK), National DV Hotline 1-800-799-7233 (US), or your local DV service, and a family lawyer experienced with DV.

## Divorce is a major life event

Among the most stressful events in adult life, alongside bereavement and serious illness. Your body knows even if your mind is trying to be strategic. Expect:
- Sleep disruption
- Appetite changes
- Immune system dip
- Mental fog
- Emotional waves (rage, grief, relief, guilt, numbness, all mixed)
- Somatic symptoms

Basics matter more in this season, not less. Sleep, food, water, gentle movement, medical care.

## Stay-or-go

When the decision hasn't been made: discernment, not action.

Questions that help:
- Have we genuinely tried to repair? Couples therapy, honest conversations, real effort?
- Is this relationship fixable, or have we both stopped trying?
- Is it safe? (Safety trumps everything.)
- Am I leaving because of them, or because I want a different life and they represent the old one?
- Am I leaving because of someone else? (Crisis decisions for another person almost never end well.)
- What do I want? Not "what do I not want" — what do I want next?
- If I imagine a year after the divorce, am I relieved or grieving?
- If I imagine a year after deciding to stay and truly committing, am I relieved or trapped?

Discernment counselling is a specific therapy for this stay-or-go phase. Worth knowing about. Different from couples therapy (which assumes both want to repair).

## Marriages that should end

Some do:
- Abuse (physical, sexual, emotional, financial, coercive control)
- Untreated addiction the partner won't address
- Serial infidelity without genuine repair
- Decades of disconnection where both have stopped trying and one or both want more
- Fundamental incompatibility where continuing is harm to both
- A partner unwilling to do their own work in any meaningful way

## Marriages that can be repaired

Some can:
- Disconnect born of busyness, children, careers — repairable with attention
- A single episode of betrayal with genuine repair and commitment
- Periods of difficulty during illness, grief, or life crisis
- Communication patterns that can be re-learned with therapy
- Loss of intimacy with both partners willing to re-engage

Knowing which you're in requires honesty and usually professional help. Don't decide alone. Don't decide from the storm.

## Once decided: slow the logistics, fast the relational

Finances, legal setups, custody arrangements: get lawyers, slow down, do it right. The most expensive decisions in divorce are the ones made quickly without advice.

The emotional conversations: don't linger in indefinite ambiguity. Both of you suffering in an extended limbo is worse than moving through the hard conversation and into clarity.

## Get a lawyer

Even for amicable divorces. Even if you think you can handle it yourselves.

Why: the decisions you make now shape the next decade or more. Custody arrangements signed in good faith don't always hold up. Financial settlements that seemed fair can turn out not to be. Pension splits that were missed cost decades of income. Insurance, tax treatment, asset division, children's schooling — all complex.

Each person ideally has their own lawyer. The cost of getting this right is much less than the cost of getting it wrong.

## Mediation vs litigation

**Mediation:** a neutral mediator + each party with their own lawyer checking the outcome. Usually faster, cheaper, less adversarial, better for ongoing co-parenting. The default unless there's a reason not to.

**Collaborative divorce:** similar idea, more structured, both sides commit not to litigate.

**Litigation:** court-based, adversarial, expensive, slow. Necessary sometimes (domestic violence, bad-faith partner hiding assets, extreme conflict around children), but worst for everyone when avoidable.

## Children

Research is clear: what predicts children's outcomes post-divorce is not the divorce itself, but the level of ongoing parental conflict.

- Low-conflict divorces: children generally do fine long-term.
- High-conflict divorces: children bear damage for decades.

The single most important post-divorce parenting task: protect the co-parenting relationship, even if the marital relationship ended badly.

**Rules to hold:**
- Never bad-mouth the other parent in front of (or within earshot of) the children
- Never use children as messengers
- Never interrogate them after visits
- Never make them choose sides
- Share custody information directly with the other parent, not through the child
- Keep schedules predictable
- Both parents at school events, medical appointments, milestones

Even when the ex is genuinely behaving badly. Your child's long-term wellbeing depends on this more than on winning any argument.

## The emotional arc

Non-linear. Expect:
- Shock
- Denial
- Relief (sometimes early, sometimes hidden)
- Grief — for the marriage, for the future imagined, for the family unit
- Rage
- Guilt
- Shame (especially around narratives of "failure")
- Identity confusion
- Loneliness
- Terror about logistics (money, housing, single parenting)
- Glimpses of the life that might come after
- Slow return to equilibrium

Not in that order. Some cycle through many times. This is normal. You are not going mad. This is what divorce feels like.

## Common traps

**Trying to stay friends immediately.** Rarely works. Space first, friendship later if ever. Forcing it usually damages both the ending and any possible future friendship.

**Decisions made in grief or rage.** Parenting schedules, financial agreements, sales of the house — these deserve cool heads. Buy time if you need to.

**Telling everyone everything.** Pick a small trusted circle. The divorce is not public property. You will regret the Facebook post. You will regret telling the dentist.

**Social media.** Nothing about the ex. Nothing about the process. Nothing about the pain. Everything you post stays. Future you, and future employers, and future partners, will see it. Stay off.

**The rebound.** Understandable. Usually painful. The research is clear: people who start serious new relationships in the first 1–2 years after divorce tend to repeat patterns they haven't yet processed. Use the single time for rebuilding.

**Using kids as confidants.** They are not your therapist. They are your children. Share with friends, therapists, support groups. Protect their relationship with both parents.

## Identity loss

Spouse is a huge part of identity. When it ends, there's a real vacuum. You may:
- Not know how to introduce yourself
- Not know what you want for dinner
- Not know what to do on weekends
- Not know who you are without the couple-framework

This is normal. It takes 1–3 years to rebuild into a life that feels fully yours. Meanwhile:
- Re-establish old friendships that atrophied during the marriage
- Try things you put down — hobbies, interests, creative work
- Live alone for a while if you can
- Travel if possible, even modestly
- Re-discover solitude

## The body

Divorce is major stress. Take it seriously:
- Sleep will be disrupted; protect it
- Appetite will shift; eat anyway
- Alcohol often rises; watch it
- Exercise drops at the exact time you need it most
- Get medical attention for any persistent symptom
- Therapy, even brief, almost always helps

## Do not date too soon

Most therapists recommend at least 1–2 years of being single before serious new relationships. This is a minimum, and it's rough guidance, not a law.

Why: the patterns that contributed to the ending have not yet been examined. The rebound partner is often startlingly like the ex, or its opposite — both are reactive, neither is chosen.

Use the single time for the inner work. Therapy. Friendships. Your relationship with yourself. The next relationship is only different if you are.

## Rebuilding is the real work

The legal divorce is the middle of the process, not the end. After the papers are signed:
- Home — new or re-shaped
- Routines — built from scratch
- Finances — often restructured
- Friendships — some reset, new ones made
- Identity — slowly integrated
- Eventually: new relationship, if wanted

This takes years. The first year after divorce is often worst in some ways (the grief lands; the legal structure is new; the community support fades). Year two, three, four — the new life takes shape.

## When therapy helps

Almost always useful during this transition. Specifically useful:
- Pre-divorce discernment (discernment counselling)
- During divorce (individual therapy for support)
- After divorce (processing, pattern work, preparing for next relationship)
- Family therapy if children are struggling
- Couples therapy with the ex sometimes useful if co-parenting conflict is high

## A weekly minimum

- Sleep protected
- One activity that is just for you, not about kids or ex
- One person you told the truth to this week
- Physical movement, most days
- No phone scrolling of ex/new partner of ex/divorce content
- Legal / logistical work in specific blocks, not bleeding into everything
- One moment of looking forward, not only backward
- Grace for yourself. You're in a hard season. Hard seasons end.
