# Health & Nutrition Principles (General Guidance)

## A note on scope

This is general nutrition guidance for healthy adults. It is not medical or dietetic advice. For anything specific to a medical condition, pregnancy, an eating disorder history, or medication, see a doctor or registered dietitian.

## The 80/20 of nutrition

You do not need a PhD in nutrition to eat well. Eighty percent of the benefit comes from a handful of durable principles:

1. Eat mostly whole foods.
2. Include protein at every meal.
3. Get enough fibre (most people don't).
4. Drink water.
5. Limit ultra-processed foods.
6. Don't over-drink alcohol.
7. Pay attention to how food makes you feel over hours, not minutes.

Do these most of the time and you will outperform almost any diet book.

## Whole foods mean

Vegetables, fruit, legumes, nuts, seeds, whole grains, eggs, dairy (if tolerated), fish, meat, herbs, spices, oils. Foods with one ingredient, or foods that could be explained to a grandparent without needing to read a label.

It doesn't mean "organic" or "clean" or any particular branded lifestyle. It just means the food looks roughly like food, not like a product designed in a lab.

## Protein

Most people under-eat protein, especially at breakfast. A useful rough target is 1.2–2.0 grams per kg of body weight per day for active adults, spread across meals.

Sources: eggs, Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, fish, chicken, meat, tofu, tempeh, legumes, protein-forward dairy. Vegetarians: combine legumes with grains and include soy foods, dairy, eggs if used.

Protein supports muscle, satiety, and stable energy. It helps across most goals.

## Fibre

Aim for 25–38g/day. Most people hit 10–15g. The gap is significant — fibre supports gut health, blood sugar stability, cholesterol, and satiety.

Easy wins: beans and lentils, oats, berries, cruciferous vegetables (broccoli, cauliflower), leafy greens, whole grains, nuts and seeds, chia, flaxseed.

If you are going from low to high fibre, go gradually and drink more water. Otherwise expect gas.

## Hydration

Water first, most of the time. Coffee and tea count toward fluid. Sugary drinks and juice are dessert, not hydration.

Simple target: pale yellow urine, thirst mostly absent during the day. Needs go up with heat, exercise, altitude, fever, caffeine.

## Ultra-processed food

The evidence is now consistent: diets high in ultra-processed food (packaged snacks, sugary drinks, fast food, many "health" bars, most breakfast cereals, processed meats) correlate with worse health outcomes across multiple measures.

You don't have to eliminate UPFs. Reducing them is one of the highest-leverage moves in nutrition. A working rule: the majority of your food should be recognisable as a plant or an animal.

## Alcohol

The research has shifted. The old "moderate drinking is protective" story does not hold up under better studies. Less is healthier. If you drink, keep it moderate, and honestly assess whether it is adding to your life or quietly costing you sleep, mood, energy, and time.

## Weight: a honest framing

Weight and body composition are influenced by many factors — genetics, hormones, medications, sleep, stress, food environment, movement, history of dieting — not just "calories in, calories out" in the oversimplified sense.

If weight change is a goal, approaches that tend to work long-term: adequate protein, adequate fibre, mostly whole foods, consistent eating rhythm, good sleep, regular movement. What rarely works long-term: extreme restriction, all-or-nothing diets, demonising food groups, ignoring satiety.

Many people do best when they stop making body size the goal and make energy, strength, and daily wellbeing the goals. Body composition often follows.

## When food becomes distressing

If food-related thoughts are taking up large parts of your day, if you are restricting intensely, if you are bingeing and restricting, or if your self-worth rises and falls with the scale — this is outside normal nutrition coaching. It is eating disorder territory and deserves specialised support.

Resources: Beat (UK), NEDA (US), your GP, or a registered dietitian trained in eating disorders.

## A weekly shape

A sustainable week of eating looks roughly like:

- **Most meals:** a protein + plenty of vegetables + some carbohydrate + some fat. Simple.
- **Snacks (if needed):** something with protein or fibre (yogurt, fruit with nuts, hummus with veg) rather than pure sugar or refined starch.
- **Drinks:** water most of the time.
- **Treats:** enjoyed without guilt, a few times a week.
- **Sleep:** treated as part of nutrition — it drives food decisions more than willpower does.

## One change at a time

Do not overhaul everything. Pick one of these and do it for 4 weeks:

- Add a source of protein to breakfast every day.
- Add a serving of vegetables to lunch and dinner.
- Drink a glass of water on waking.
- Stop eating after 8pm on weekdays.
- Reduce alcohol by half.

Small consistent changes beat dramatic overhauls that collapse in week three.
