# Authentic Relationships Principles

## The foundation: who you are, not what you do

You cannot strategise your way into a genuine, aligned relationship. Tactics, game, optimisation, and "high-value" performance lead to relationships with people drawn to those performances — not to you.

The work is to become the kind of person who naturally attracts aligned partners. That means:
- knowing yourself
- owning your needs and standards
- living in integrity with your values
- being a whole life someone joins, not half a life seeking a completer

When you are that, alignment shows up differently. And when it shows up, you recognise it — because it feels like ease and clarity, not chase and uncertainty.

## Clarity on values beats a list of traits

Most people making "dream partner" lists focus on traits: tall, funny, successful, good-looking, likes dogs.

Traits tell you who you're attracted to. Values tell you who you can build a life with. The two are different questions.

Values include:
- What kind of life do you want to build? (Rooted and quiet, or adventurous and mobile? Suburban, urban, rural?)
- How do you want to handle money, conflict, family, work?
- Do you want children?
- What role does spirituality, tradition, politics play?
- How do you spend weekends? Holidays? Sundays?

Shared values predict long-term compatibility. Traits just predict initial attraction. Many painful relationships were strong on traits and catastrophic on values.

## Non-negotiables, spoken and kept

Before dating anyone, know your genuine non-negotiables. Not preferences. Deal-breakers.

Common ones: wants children or doesn't, sober or drinks, religion, political alignment where it affects daily life, core values around honesty, willingness to do their own emotional work, geographic constraints.

These are not controlling. They are honest. If you don't know them, you will discover them painfully six months in. If you know them and ignore them hoping the person will change, you will suffer.

Write them down. Keep them.

## Attraction is data, not destiny

Strong chemistry does not mean compatibility. Often it means familiarity — the other person's patterns feel like home because they match wounds from your own history.

The "instant spark" is often the wound recognising the wound. Slow chemistry that deepens over months is often much safer ground for long-term partnership than fireworks in week one.

Notice: is the attraction pulling me toward someone who is actually good for me, or toward someone who fits an old pattern? If you keep ending up in the same painful dynamic with different people, attraction is your data, not your guide.

## Availability is the filter

Consistent, emotionally available behaviour is the single most reliable signal of a partner who can actually build something with you. No amount of chemistry, charisma, or potential overrides it.

**Available looks like:** shows up when they say they will, answers messages without games, doesn't disappear, can talk about feelings, handles conflict without flaming out, works on themselves, is actually single and ready for a relationship.

**Unavailable looks like:** hot and cold, hard to pin down, has an ex situation, is in a difficult season they say they need time to get through, says they're "not ready for a relationship but really like you", disappears and returns, makes you chase.

Unavailability is not a fixer-upper project. It is a clear no, spoken in behaviour. Read the behaviour, not the words.

## Self-worth as the floor

Your self-worth sets what you tolerate, not what you attract. A person with a high floor does not tolerate disrespect, disappearance, or dishonesty — they simply step away when it shows up. A person with a low floor accepts much and calls it patience.

You do not rise toward a partner by being more appealing. You set the floor by what you walk away from.

This is the hardest work: being willing to end it when someone is actually not treating you well, even when you want them, even when chemistry is strong, even when you're afraid of being alone.

## The "already partnered" self

Ask: if a wonderful aligned relationship were already here, what would my life look like?

- Where would I live?
- What would I do with my time?
- What would I be working on?
- Who would I be around?
- What would I have stopped doing?

Start living as that person now. Not because "the universe will send your match" when you do — though it might. Because you will build a life that is rich and whole regardless, and any relationship that arrives will be an addition to a life already worth living, not a rescue from one that isn't.

This is also the best filter. People who are drawn to a rich, grounded life are usually people who are bringing one. People drawn to someone they can rescue (or be rescued by) are usually in a different category.

## Release as a practice

Much of this work is letting go — of specific people, of old patterns, of fantasies about the one that got away, of dynamics that looked promising and weren't.

Release looks like:
- Grieving the person you wanted it to be.
- Naming what you learned from them.
- Ceremonially closing it (a letter not sent, a walk, a burning, whatever fits).
- Stopping contact if that serves your healing.
- Redirecting attention to your own life.

What you release makes space. What you cling to blocks the channel. This is not spiritual wishful thinking — it is practical. You cannot meet new people with genuine openness while half your heart is with someone from the past.

## The patterns worth noticing

Watch for these recurring shapes:

- **Chasing the avoidant.** You pursue, they withdraw, you pursue harder. Classic anxious-avoidant dance.
- **Being the fixer.** You date people who need saving, feel special because they need you, resent them when they don't change.
- **Settling out of scarcity.** "No one else will want me, so this will do" — the foundation of many long unhappy relationships.
- **Choosing potential over reality.** You fall for who they could be if they did the work, rather than who they are.
- **Serial monogamy without breaks.** Never being alone long enough to know yourself between relationships.

If you see yourself in one of these, the repair is patience: break the pattern once, consciously, even if it costs a specific person. The next relationship is different only if you are.

## Dating as practice, not audition

Dating is practice — meeting people, conversing, noticing what you feel and value. Not an audition in which you perform to be chosen.

Go on dates as yourself. Notice how you feel in their presence. Ask real questions. Give real answers. Do not hide your quirks, your ambitions, your concerns, your deal-breakers.

The right person will be drawn in by exactly who you are. Everyone else is doing you a favour by not being drawn in.

## When to consider therapy

If you:
- Repeat the same painful dynamic with multiple partners
- Attract people with patterns similar to a difficult parent
- Struggle with anxious or avoidant attachment at an intensity that disrupts daily life
- Have unprocessed trauma from a past relationship
- Find yourself unable to leave relationships you know are bad for you

Consider working with a therapist alongside this coaching. Attachment patterns are often deep and are often best worked with somatic or depth-psychological approaches, not coaching alone.

## A weekly practice

- One act of building the life you'd want whether or not a relationship is here yet.
- One release of something old — a name, an old message thread, a fantasy.
- One honest check-in: am I living my non-negotiables, or softening them out of loneliness?
- One practice of self-worth in action: say no to something that didn't meet your floor; notice the discomfort; stay.
