# Highly Sensitive Person (HSP) Principles

## What HSP actually means

Highly Sensitive Person (HSP) refers to a measurable neurobiological trait — Sensory Processing Sensitivity (SPS) — identified by Elaine Aron. About 20% of the population meets the criteria. The trait is found in ~100 species, not just humans, suggesting it's a stable evolutionary strategy.

HSP is not a disorder. It is a trait, like introversion or openness to experience. The brain is wired to process sensory, social, and emotional input more deeply. Every HSP thing — the exhaustion from crowds, the overwhelm from loud music, the noticing of small details, the emotional sensitivity — follows from this.

## Not all HSPs are the same

Common variants:
- **Introverted HSP (~70%):** recharges alone, overwhelmed by social intensity.
- **Extroverted HSP (~30%):** energised by people but still overwhelmed by sensory intensity.
- **Highly Sensitive Sensation-Seeker (HSS-HSP):** both sensitivity and a drive toward novelty and intensity. Internally contradictory and often exhausting.

HSP also overlaps significantly with:
- Autism (sensitivity is a feature)
- ADHD (sensory sensitivity is common)
- Giftedness (overexcitabilities)
- Synesthesia
- Trauma history (which can mimic trait sensitivity)

If the above overlaps apply, working with those frames may be more useful than HSP alone. The HSP frame is not the only way to describe this.

## The D.O.E.S. framework

Aron's description of what defines the trait:

**D — Depth of processing.**
You think more about things. Make more connections. Analyse, reflect, notice subtle implications. Useful and exhausting.

**O — Overstimulation.**
Bright lights, loud sounds, crowds, fast-moving environments, too many decisions, social density — you reach saturation faster than non-HSPs.

**E — Emotional responsiveness and empathy.**
You feel your own emotions deeply. You also pick up others' emotions — sometimes without realising they aren't yours.

**S — Sensitivity to subtle stimuli.**
You notice what others miss: the shift in someone's tone, a detail in a room, a small change in the weather, a pattern others don't see.

All four usually present. Manage them as a set.

## The trauma question

Aron herself is clear: trait HSP and trauma-related hypersensitivity can look similar, but they are different.

- **Trait HSP:** has been you all your life. Stable. Doesn't come from a specific event.
- **Trauma response:** often started after a specific period. Accompanied by hypervigilance, startle response, intrusive memories, dissociation, flashbacks. More all-over activation; harder to calm.

Many people have both. Trauma-informed support (therapist, somatic practitioner) is often the right frame if trauma is present, regardless of whether the person also fits HSP.

## It is not a flaw

Your whole life, you have probably been told:
- "You're too sensitive"
- "Don't take things so personally"
- "Toughen up"
- "You're overreacting"
- "Why does this bother you so much when it doesn't bother anyone else?"

These are not feedback about your flaws. They are feedback about the mismatch between your nervous system and the environment. Both can change; the environment usually more than you.

The trait is protective and useful in the right context. It becomes disabling only when the environment is relentlessly overstimulating and there's no recovery.

## Recovery is not a luxury

HSPs genuinely need more downtime than non-HSPs. This is biological, not psychological.

Without adequate recovery:
- Overstimulation accumulates
- Mood drops
- Decision quality drops
- Physical symptoms appear
- Empathy becomes overwhelm
- Everything gets harder

Required:
- Solitude (varies by person — an introverted HSP may need hours daily; an extroverted HSP may need less but still some)
- Quiet
- Unstructured time
- Nature
- Slower pace
- Sleep (HSPs often need more, not less)

Schedule these deliberately. They're not self-indulgence. They're your nervous system's maintenance.

## Environment design

HSPs are affected by environment more than most. Design deliberately:

**At home:**
- Low-clutter visual space (visual clutter = attention tax)
- Natural light where possible
- Quiet corners you can retreat to
- Plants and nature elements
- Soft lighting in the evening
- A bedroom that's truly restful

**At work:**
- Open-plan offices are genuinely harder for HSPs. If you can negotiate an office, remote work, or a quiet corner, do.
- Headphones, earplugs, earbuds — not avoidance, just dampening.
- Natural light, view if possible.
- Break frequently; short walks.

**In social life:**
- Smaller gatherings over large ones
- One-on-one or small-group deep conversation over cocktail parties
- Earlier events, shorter stays
- Exits from events that don't require drama
- Recovery time after intense social days

## Social dosing

HSPs aren't necessarily antisocial. They just need a different form:
- Long, deep conversations with one person — fine.
- Loud bar with 10 people — exhausting.
- Weekend with close family — manageable in small doses.
- Large party with small talk — punishing.

Know your forms. Choose them deliberately. Decline politely and often.

## The processing lag

HSPs often can't respond in the moment. The clever comeback, the boundary, the decision — you need time to process.

- "Let me think about that and get back to you" — a full sentence.
- Write your response to an emotional email tomorrow, not now.
- Big decisions: let them sit several days.
- Don't feel obliged to have answers immediately.

Claim the processing lag as a feature, not a weakness.

## Empath or enmeshed?

Many HSPs pick up others' emotions as if they were their own. This is not mystical — it's heightened emotional contagion plus deep processing.

Distinguish:
- **Is this mine?** Sometimes the feeling is yours.
- **Am I carrying it?** Sometimes it's picked up from someone else.

If it arrived when you entered a room, when you spoke to a specific person, when the news came on — it may not be yours. You can set it down.

Boundaries here are not about caring less. They are about not losing yourself in others. You can feel with someone without becoming them.

## Nervous system tools

Because HSPs activate faster, regulation tools matter more.

**Daily practices that help:**
- Morning quiet (no phone, no news, no input first thing)
- Mindfulness or meditation (even 5 minutes)
- Walking, especially in nature
- Breathwork (extended exhale)
- Creative or making time (outlet, not output)
- Adequate sleep (often 8+ hours needed)
- Regular meals (low blood sugar hits HSPs harder)
- Less caffeine (caffeine sensitivity is often higher)

**Before intense events:**
- Grounding practice
- Eat properly
- Plan an exit
- Limit caffeine that day

**After intense events:**
- Unstructured quiet time
- Walk
- Reduced demands for a few hours

## Strengths to claim

The sensitivity that costs you also gives you:
- Attention to detail and depth
- Empathy others lack
- Pattern recognition
- Conscientiousness
- Creative capacity (many creatives are HSP)
- Spiritual / contemplative capacity
- Good instinct for what's off in a room or situation
- Deep loyalty and capacity for connection
- Aesthetic sensibility

These are not consolation prizes. They are real gifts that many non-HSP people don't have. The trait makes you suited for certain things. Let those things be yours.

## What depletes HSPs most

- Violent / intense media (films, news, graphic content)
- Conflict, especially unresolved
- Time pressure
- Too many decisions
- Multitasking
- Open-plan offices
- Loud restaurants / bars
- Dysregulated people (especially unavoidably — angry partner, chaotic boss, distressed relative)
- Socially dense schedules with no recovery
- Hunger, thirst, lack of sleep (amplifies everything)

Remove what you can. Protect against what you can't.

## What nourishes HSPs

- Nature, especially quiet nature
- Beauty — art, music, architecture
- Deep one-on-one conversation
- Creative making
- Animals
- Slow food, slow walks, slow days
- Solitude without guilt
- Sleep in a dark, quiet room
- Time with other sensitive people
- Meaningful work

Prioritise these. They're not decoration. They're what keeps you well.

## Boundaries

HSPs often struggle with saying no because they feel the other person's disappointment intensely. This leads to chronic over-commitment and burnout.

Practice:
- "That doesn't work for me"
- "I'll need to check my energy and get back to you"
- "No, thank you" (full sentence)

Feel the discomfort of someone else's disappointment without rushing to fix it. This is the core boundary skill. Build it slowly.

## When to seek support

- If depression, anxiety, or overwhelm are persistent beyond good self-care
- If trauma history is a factor
- If the sensitivity pattern started recently or suddenly (trauma, medical issue, life event worth investigating)
- If relationships are chronically burning you out
- If you can't imagine designing a life that works for your nervous system

A therapist — ideally one who understands sensitivity, HSP, or trauma — can help enormously. This is not weakness. It's the right tool for the shape of the challenge.

## A weekly rhythm

- Morning quiet
- Movement in nature at least twice
- One deep one-on-one conversation
- At least one unstructured low-input evening
- Sleep protected
- Sensory diet managed (less phone, less news, less noise)
- Creative / contemplative time
- No apologising for needing what you need

This trait is not a problem to solve. It is a way of being in the world. Design accordingly.
