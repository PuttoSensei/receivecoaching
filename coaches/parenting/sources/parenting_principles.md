# Parenting Principles

## The parent's state is the biggest lever

You cannot co-regulate a child from a dysregulated state. When you are exhausted, resentful, or running hot, your parenting becomes reactive no matter what technique you know. The single biggest investment in being a better parent is tending to yourself — sleep, support, solitude, your own regulation.

Most "parenting problems" dissolve when the parent is rested, supported, and has some room. Most "parenting techniques" fail when they don't.

## Connection before correction

A child in dysregulation cannot learn from consequences. Their brain is in survival mode. Teaching, lecturing, or punishing in that state does nothing except damage the relationship.

The order is: calm the body, restore connection, then (if needed) address the behaviour. Sometimes once connection is restored, the behaviour doesn't need addressing. Sometimes it does, and the conversation goes much better.

## Repair matters more than perfection

You will lose your patience. You will say the wrong thing. You will react instead of respond. This is not the end of anything.

What matters is what happens next. Going back: "I yelled at you. I wish I hadn't. That wasn't fair to you. I love you. I'm working on it." This single practice, repeated, protects the long-term relationship more than any technique.

Children raised by parents who repair after rupture grow up knowing conflict is survivable and relationships can be mended. That is one of the greatest gifts of good-enough parenting.

## Stage-appropriate expectations

A two-year-old cannot self-regulate. A seven-year-old cannot reliably think about long-term consequences. A fourteen-year-old is developmentally wired to push against you — it is their job. Expecting adult reasoning from a pre-adult brain causes endless frustration.

Know what your child's brain can and cannot do at their stage. Parent to what's actually there, not what you wish were there.

Rough map:
- **0–1:** regulation, responsiveness. The baby cannot be spoiled by attention.
- **1–3:** big feelings, small bodies. No reasoning with a toddler in a meltdown.
- **3–7:** magical thinking, imagination, simple cause-and-effect. Stories and play are how they learn.
- **7–11:** reason emerges. They can understand consequences. Peer influence begins.
- **11–13:** identity work starts. Privacy matters. Moods swing.
- **13–18:** individuation is developmental, not personal. They need to push against you to find themselves. Be the lighthouse.
- **18+:** the relationship shifts to peer-like. Unsolicited advice damages. Presence serves.

## Name the feeling

When a child is in a storm of emotion, naming the feeling for them is regulating. "You're really angry. You wanted to keep playing and now we have to go. That's hard."

You are not agreeing with their position. You are making their inner state legible. Children who are named through their emotions learn to name their own.

This beats reasoning, bargaining, distracting, or punishing in almost every case.

## Hold limits warmly

You can hold a firm boundary without warmth (tyrannical) or with warmth without firmness (permissive). Both damage. The middle is: warm AND firm.

"I understand you want it. The answer is still no. I know that's disappointing. I love you. The answer is still no."

No negotiation. No guilt. No withholding of love. Just the limit, held kindly.

## You do not have to win

Parenting is not a contest. If your child has a meltdown and you stay regulated, you haven't lost. You have done the work. The meltdown is a nervous system event; your regulation teaches theirs over time.

The parent who needs to win every power struggle with a 3-year-old produces tired parents and wounded children. Drop the need to win. Hold the limits you actually care about. Let the rest go.

## Specific to teens

The teen years are about individuation. The disagreement, the eye-rolling, the challenging of your rules and views — this is developmental. If they are not pushing against you in some form, something is wrong.

The work:
- Be the lighthouse, not the coastguard. Stand steady. Let them come and go.
- Pick your battles. Many. Let most things go.
- Don't take it personally. It's not about you.
- Keep the relationship alive even when it's prickly. Shared meals, car rides, being available.
- Trust will be tested. Hold the limits that matter (safety, honesty, respect) and release the ones that don't (clothing, music, their mess).

## Siblings and comparison

Never compare them. Siblings fight; that's normal. Trying to make them love each other by commanding it backfires. Protect each child's individual relationship with you.

## Screens and technology

Current best evidence: delayed smartphones (later is better), no phone in the bedroom, clear limits on time and content, and your own example matters enormously. A parent on their phone during dinner teaches something the lecture doesn't undo.

## Play is not a luxury

Play is how children integrate experience, regulate emotion, and develop executive function. Under-scheduled kids are not falling behind; over-scheduled kids are depleted. Protect play, especially free play.

## When to escalate

See a professional (pediatrician, child psychologist, family therapist, school counsellor) if:
- Persistent changes in mood, appetite, sleep
- Withdrawal, self-harm signs, talk of not wanting to be alive
- Significant behavioural changes lasting weeks
- Signs of trauma (regression, nightmares, hypervigilance)
- School refusal
- Eating disorder signs
- Suspicion of developmental difference (autism, ADHD, learning difference)

Getting help early is not an overreaction; it is good parenting.

## Parent yourself

The patterns you have with your children are often the patterns your parents had with you. Not identically — but often. Unfelt feelings from your own childhood tend to be triggered by your children's feelings. Rage that flashes too easily, shame that closes you down, the urge to be nothing like your mother while being exactly like her.

Your own therapy, reflection, or parenting-focused work pays dividends to your children. A parent doing their own inner work is giving their child a future-self less weighed down by inherited patterns.

## The weekly minimum

- One-on-one time with each child, however brief, unstructured
- A meal together most days
- A nightly check-in that isn't homework or behaviour
- Your own rest
- Your own support (partner, friends, community)
- Grace when you got it wrong, and the repair the next day
