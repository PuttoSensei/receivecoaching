# NLP Techniques & Principles

## What NLP actually is (honestly)

Neuro-Linguistic Programming is a toolkit developed in the 1970s by Bandler and Grinder from modelling effective therapists. Its scientific claims about the brain are not well-supported. Its practical techniques — reframing, pattern interruption, outcome-setting, language questioning — are genuinely useful for shifting stuck states and stuck language.

Use the tools. Ignore the pseudoscientific packaging.

## The map is not the territory

Your internal representation of a situation is not the situation itself. It is a map — shaped by language, past experience, and current state. Two people in the same situation have very different maps. Most emotional suffering is a map problem, not a territory problem.

The coaching question: *what map are you currently using, and is it the most useful one?*

## Well-formed outcomes

A goal is only useful if it is well-formed. Five checks:

1. **Positive** — what you *want*, not what you want to stop. "Not be anxious" is not a goal. "Be calm and focused in meetings" is.
2. **Specific and sensory** — how will you know you have it? What will you see, hear, feel?
3. **Self-initiated** — within your control. "My partner changes" is not a goal. "I communicate what I need clearly" is.
4. **Contextualised** — when, where, with whom, how often.
5. **Ecological** — what does having it cost? What secondary gain do you get from *not* having it? (If there's a hidden benefit to staying stuck, the goal will not hold.)

Run any goal through these five before committing.

## The meta-model

Language simplifies reality. Three common simplifications — and their counters:

### Generalisations
- "She never listens." → *Never? Can you think of one time she did?*
- "Everyone thinks I'm bad at this." → *Everyone? Who specifically?*

### Deletions
- "I'm confused." → *Confused about what, specifically?*
- "They said no." → *Who, specifically? Said what, specifically?*

### Distortions
- "He makes me feel worthless." → *How, specifically, does what he does cause the feeling of worthless in you?*
- "I know she's judging me." → *How do you know that is what she is thinking?*

The meta-model is not an interrogation. It is gentle precision. Each question opens the user's map a little further.

## Reframing

A reframe changes the meaning or context of an experience without changing the experience itself.

- **Meaning reframe:** "You call it stubborn. Could you also call it committed?"
- **Context reframe:** "That behaviour is a problem at work. Where in your life would it be a strength?"

Reframes work when they are true and fit. They fail when they are forced or used to dismiss real pain. The test: does the user lean in, or flinch?

## State change

You cannot think your way out of a state you are stuck in. You can move your way out.

- **Breath:** four seconds in, eight seconds out, for two minutes. Downshifts the nervous system.
- **Movement:** stand up, walk, shake hands out, change rooms.
- **Sensory shift:** look at something far away for thirty seconds if you've been staring at a screen. Listen to music that matches the state you want, not the one you're in.
- **Physiology:** posture shifts state. Stand tall, breathe from the belly, unclench jaw.

Do any of these *before* trying to reframe cognitively. The brain does not reason its way out of a dysregulated body.

## Anchoring

An anchor is a deliberate association between a stimulus and a state. Most anchors happen accidentally — a song takes you back, a smell returns you to childhood. You can install useful ones deliberately:

1. Recall a specific moment when you were deeply in the resource state you want (calm, confident, focused).
2. Re-enter that memory fully — see what you saw, hear what you heard, feel what you felt.
3. At the peak of the state, apply a unique physical anchor (press thumb and middle finger together firmly).
4. Repeat 3–5 times in different sessions.
5. Trigger the anchor when you need the state.

It is not magic. It is classical conditioning applied to yourself. Works for some people, partially for others.

## Pattern interrupts

When a thought loop is running, breaking the pattern is often easier than reasoning with it. Stand up mid-sentence. Clap. Change rooms. Say something absurd out loud. The loop breaks. Now you have a choice.

## Presuppositions worth holding

Not claims about reality, but useful operating assumptions:

- *Behind every behaviour is a positive intention*, even if the behaviour itself is destructive.
- *People are not their behaviour.* The behaviour is what they did; the person is more than that.
- *There is no failure, only feedback.* Every outcome is information.
- *The person with the most flexibility in a system influences the system most.*

These are not universally true. They are useful to act as if they were, because they open options.
