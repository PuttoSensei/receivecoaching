# Procrastination Principles

## Procrastination is emotional, not organisational

The single most important insight: procrastination is not a time management problem. It is an emotional regulation problem.

You procrastinate on tasks that trigger uncomfortable feelings — fear, boredom, overwhelm, resentment, shame. The brain ducks the feeling by doing something else. It is not laziness; it is emotional avoidance disguised as poor planning.

Time management systems fail procrastinators because the problem is upstream of planning. Fix the emotional dynamic and the planning usually handles itself.

## The different kinds (each needs a different tool)

Not all procrastination is the same. Diagnose before treating:

### Perfectionist procrastination
*The task feels too important to start imperfectly.*

You want the result to be great. Starting imperfectly feels unbearable. So you don't start.

**The fix:** lower the bar radically. The first draft is meant to be bad. Allow yourself to be bad at it on purpose. "Shitty first draft" is the whole method. You cannot improve what doesn't exist.

### Overwhelm procrastination
*The task feels too big to begin.*

You can't see the end, so you can't start. The whole project sits in your head like a wall.

**The fix:** shrink it. Not "plan the project." The smallest physical next action. Not "write the book" — "open the document." Not "apply for jobs" — "draft one sentence of the cover letter." Tiny enough to start without thinking.

### Avoidance procrastination
*The task triggers a specific fear.*

Fear of rejection. Of failure. Of finding out something you don't want to know. Of a specific uncomfortable conversation.

**The fix:** name the fear. Often reduces it. Then graded exposure: do the smallest, lowest-stakes version of the scary thing. Send one easy email. Have one five-minute check-in before the full meeting. Build tolerance.

### Boredom procrastination
*The task is boring.*

Admin. Data entry. Repetitive work. The brain has no dopamine here and keeps wandering to more interesting things.

**The fix:** make it less boring — music, a body double, a change of environment, a time-box with a small reward at the end. Or batch it: do all the boring tasks in one designated block so the rest of the day is interesting.

### Resentment procrastination
*You don't want to do it because it feels imposed.*

Someone else's priority. A commitment you said yes to but don't actually want. A task that feels like it shouldn't be yours.

**The fix:** either renegotiate (genuinely don't do it, with consequences) or consciously choose it. "I am choosing to do this because..." converts it from imposition to choice. Internal rebellion fades once there is no authority to rebel against.

### Structural procrastination (Perry's concept)
*You do lots of productive things instead of the one important thing.*

You clean the house. You reply to emails. You reorganise your tasks. You get actual work done — just not the work that matters.

**The fix:** the productive avoidance is the tell. Name it: "I'm avoiding X by doing Y, which is productive but not what matters." Then: the smallest next step on X.

### Decision procrastination
*You don't know what to do next, so you do nothing.*

Which task first? Which project? Which option? The analysis becomes the avoidance.

**The fix:** any decision beats paralysis. Pick the least bad option and start. Course-correct later. Motion creates clarity; rumination doesn't.

## Start small. Absurdly small.

The 2-minute rule in reverse: whatever you're avoiding, define a 2-minute version and commit to just that.

- Writing a report? Open the document. Type one sentence. That's it. You can stop.
- Cleaning the garage? Pick up one object. Put it where it goes. You can stop.
- Going for a run? Put on the shoes. Go to the door. You can stop.

Almost always, once started, you continue. Starting is the bottleneck, not finishing. Once the brain is engaged, momentum carries. The ultra-small commitment is the bridge across the activation energy.

## The 5-minute timer

Set a timer for 5 minutes. Work only on the avoided task until the timer goes. You can stop when it ends.

You almost never stop. Once started, the task is less aversive than expected. The brain updates: "this wasn't as bad as I thought." Next time is slightly easier.

The research-backed technique for breaking the avoidance loop.

## Implementation intentions

Vague: "I'll work on it this week."
Specific: "On Tuesday at 10am, I will sit at my desk, open the document, and write for 30 minutes."

The specific version works; the vague one doesn't. Implementation intentions (Gollwitzer's research) beat willpower in study after study. Pin the when, where, what.

## Body doubling

Working alongside someone else, even in silence, even on unrelated tasks, dramatically increases follow-through. In-person, video call, or virtual body-doubling apps. It works because human attention helps regulate attention.

Extremely effective for procrastinators and for ADHD brains. Underused by everyone else.

## Environment design

Shape the environment so the right thing is easier than the wrong thing.

- The project open and visible on your desk.
- The phone in another room.
- App blockers running.
- Workout clothes laid out the night before.
- The journal on the pillow.

Friction determines behaviour more reliably than motivation. Remove friction from what you want to do; add friction to what you're avoiding by doing instead.

## The shame spiral

The secondary problem of procrastination is shame. You didn't do the thing. Now you're ashamed. The shame makes the task bigger. Which makes it harder to start. Which deepens the shame.

Breaking this requires active un-shaming:
- "Procrastinators are not lazy. I am avoiding something uncomfortable. That is human."
- "Today, not yesterday. What's the next small step?"
- "Shame makes it worse. Compassion and small action make it better."

Shame is fuel for procrastination, not its cure.

## Finishing is a separate skill

Many people can finish things they've started. Others struggle more with finishing than starting. If finishing is your issue:

- The "90% finished" trap — the last 10% is often the hardest. Plan for it.
- Ship the imperfect version rather than eternal refinement.
- Externally commit to a deadline with consequences (tell someone, pay for something, book the thing).
- Small wins of finishing: do tiny projects first to build the finishing muscle.

## When procrastination is a symptom of something bigger

Consider:

- **Undiagnosed ADHD** — if the procrastination has been lifelong across all domains, not tied to specific kinds of tasks. The tools in the ADHD coach are different and more effective for this.
- **Depression** — if energy is flat across the board, nothing feels rewarding, and the procrastination is part of a bigger pattern of not doing things. Refer to medical support.
- **Burnout** — you've been pushing too long. Procrastination is your body refusing. Rest, not more effort.
- **Wrong work** — persistent procrastination on work you used to do easily may be signalling that this work is no longer right for you. Take the signal seriously.

## A weekly practice

- Each Monday: pick the ONE thing you're most tempted to procrastinate on this week. Name it.
- Each day: 5 minutes on it, minimum. More if you're in flow.
- Each Friday: what did I do? What did I avoid? What kind of procrastination was it? What tool next week?

Over months, the pattern weakens. You cannot defeat procrastination permanently — it returns whenever a hard task appears. But you can get better at meeting it when it arrives.
