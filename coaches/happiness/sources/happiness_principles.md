# Happiness & Wellbeing Principles

## Pleasure vs meaning

Pleasure is fast, fades fast, and habituates — the second slice of cake is less good than the first. Meaning is slower to build, harder to measure, and does not fade in the same way. A life heavy on pleasure and light on meaning feels thin. A life with both feels full.

Examples of meaning: making something, caring for someone, mastering something difficult, belonging to a group, serving something larger than you. Examples of pleasure: food, entertainment, comfort, novelty. You need both, but meaning is the floor.

## Hedonic adaptation

Whatever good thing you are waiting for — the raise, the move, the relationship, the body — you will adapt to it within months. The brain is a delta machine, not a level machine. It measures changes, not states.

This is not a reason to stop pursuing good things. It is a reason to stop betting your happiness on them. The durable sources of wellbeing are the ones that renew: connection, creativity, movement, service, presence. These resist adaptation because they are practices, not acquisitions.

## Make room for the hard feelings

Suppressed emotion does not disappear — it seeps. Sadness you refuse to feel becomes irritability. Fear you will not name becomes micromanaging. Grief you outrun becomes numbness.

The paradox: the fastest route to feeling better is often letting yourself feel worse first, fully, for a few minutes. Name it. Locate it in your body. Let it move. Then act.

## The three-minute rule

If you want to shift a mood, three minutes of the right thing usually works: a walk outside, a phone call to someone you love, playing a song you remember loving, writing down three specific things you noticed today. Not affirmations. Not thought-reframing. Concrete sensory or relational input.

## Connection is the biggest lever

The single most reliable predictor of wellbeing across decades of research is the quality of your close relationships. Not number. Not network. Quality. One or two people you can be honest with outperforms hundreds of acquaintances.

If you are chronically unhappy, check this first. Who do you call when something goes well? Who do you call when something goes badly? If the list is empty or feels performative, this is where the work is.

## Values-aligned action

You are happiest when what you do today matches what you say matters. Mismatch between stated values and actual allocation of time is a reliable source of unease. You do not have to live up to every value every day. But weekly and monthly, the map and the territory should roughly match.

## Small and often beats big and rare

A daily ten-minute walk outperforms a yearly two-week holiday for wellbeing. A weekly call with a friend outperforms an annual reunion. A five-minute journal outperforms a life-planning retreat. The mind is built on repetition, not events.

## Stop waiting

\"I'll be happy when...\" is the quietest trap. It postpones your life indefinitely, because the next \"when\" is always already forming. The practice is to ask, today, with what is actually in front of you: what would a good version of today look like, within these constraints? Then do one small piece of that.
