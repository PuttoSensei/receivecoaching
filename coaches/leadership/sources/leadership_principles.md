# Leadership Principles

## Your output = your team's output

The fundamental shift from individual contributor to leader. You used to be measured by what you produced. Now you are measured by what your team produces.

This is the hardest adjustment for strong ICs. Your own hands-on work matters less than how well the team you're responsible for performs. The work of a leader is enabling, unblocking, deciding, coaching, hiring, and occasionally subtracting — not primarily producing.

If you are still doing most of your old IC work as a leader, you're either a reluctant leader, a poor delegator, or in the wrong role.

## The most important skill: the one-on-one

Weekly or bi-weekly, 30–60 minutes, with every direct report.

The format that works:
1. **Their agenda first.** What's on their mind? What do they want to talk about? This is their time.
2. **Your agenda second.** Feedback, questions, things you need.
3. **Career/development occasionally.** Not every meeting. But regularly.
4. **Never skip.** Cancelling sends a message. Reschedule, don't cancel.

One-on-ones done well cover most management needs. Skipping them is where leaders fail.

## Feedback as a regular practice, not an event

The annual review is dead. Feedback should be small, specific, timely, and frequent.

- **Small:** one thing, in the moment or shortly after.
- **Specific:** "In the meeting today, when you interrupted Sarah, I noticed she stopped contributing" beats "you're dominating discussions."
- **Timely:** within 24–48 hours, while it's fresh.
- **Frequent:** dozens of small feedbacks a year, not two annual ones.

Positive feedback especially. Humans are starved for specific recognition. Provide it freely and honestly.

## Radical candor: care AND challenge

Kim Scott's frame. Feedback requires two things simultaneously: caring personally about the person, and challenging them directly.

- Care without challenge = ruinous empathy. You're nice but people don't grow.
- Challenge without care = obnoxious aggression. People flinch and avoid you.
- Neither = manipulative insincerity.
- Both = radical candor.

If the person knows you care, they can hear hard feedback. If you care but don't challenge, you're failing them.

## Delegation is a skill

Common failures:
- **Don't delegate because it's faster yourself.** Short-term true. Long-term fatal. The team never grows; you never scale.
- **Delegate tasks but not authority.** The person has responsibility without power to decide. Frustrating for them, slow for everyone.
- **Delegate then second-guess.** Undermines trust, defeats the purpose.
- **Delegate to the wrong person.** Know your team's capabilities honestly.
- **Delegate the uninteresting parts only.** You keep the fun work; they get the scraps. They notice.

Good delegation: clear outcome, clear authority, clear support available, clear check-in points, then let them work.

## Psychological safety

Amy Edmondson's research: the single strongest predictor of high-performing teams. People can:
- Speak up
- Disagree
- Raise concerns
- Admit mistakes
- Ask questions

Without punishment. Teams without this produce worse results, hide problems until they're catastrophic, and burn out talented people.

You create psychological safety primarily by how you respond to bad news. If you blow up when someone tells you something hard, you've just killed safety for everyone watching. Reward the messenger even when the message is unwelcome.

## Hire slow, fire not-so-slow

Hire carefully. The cost of a bad hire is six months of damage plus the firing process. Work samples, reference calls, scenarios over gut feels.

But "fire slow" is usually a cover for avoiding the conversation. Most managers leave underperformers too long. The team knows. The underperformer often knows at some level. The delay is:
- Costing the team morale
- Costing the underperformer dignity (they're failing while you watch)
- Costing you credibility
- Costing the organisation

When you know, act. Performance improvement plans where genuinely useful; quick exit where clearly not a fit.

## The hard conversation

The conversation you've been avoiding is usually the most important thing on your plate this week. Performance, firing, feedback, renegotiating scope, saying no to a stakeholder.

Avoidance is almost never the right call. The situation is usually worse after another month of avoidance. The discomfort of the conversation is smaller than the compounding cost of not having it.

Rule of thumb: if a conversation has been on your mental list for two weeks, have it this week.

## Standards without tyranny

Hold a high bar. Also respect the humans meeting it.

- Soft on people, hard on standards. Not hard on people, soft on standards.
- Specific expectations, specific feedback.
- Grace for mistakes; clarity that the mistake shouldn't repeat.
- Praise effort but require outcomes.

The manager who lowers standards "to be nice" produces mediocre teams and builds resentment among the people trying hard. Niceness that compromises standards is actually cruelty to your best performers.

## Manage yourself first

Your team watches how you handle stress, failure, conflict, criticism, hard decisions. You set the emotional weather.

- Dysregulated leader = dysregulated team.
- Anxious leader = anxious team.
- Petty leader = petty team.
- Steady leader = steady team.

Your own therapy, coaching, regulation practices, and friendships matter enormously. You cannot coach a team past what you've worked through in yourself.

## Managing up

Leadership also means navigating upward — your own manager, executive sponsors, stakeholders.

- Make your manager's life easier, not harder. Do their homework before asking for their time.
- Surface problems with proposed solutions, not just complaints.
- Proactively update; don't wait to be asked.
- Pick your battles. Push back on things that matter. Let small stuff go.
- Understand their pressures. Their fires are not always your fires.

## Communicating difficult news

When you have to announce something hard — layoffs, a failed project, missed targets:
- Tell them the truth.
- Tell them what you know and what you don't.
- Tell them what happens next and when they'll hear more.
- Let them ask questions.
- Show up; don't hide behind email.

People can handle hard news. They cannot handle being lied to, left in the dark, or treated as if they can't handle the truth.

## Meetings

Reform your meetings or they will eat your role.
- Every meeting needs a purpose and an output.
- Prefer async for information sharing.
- Use meetings for decisions, conversations, and alignment.
- Short by default — 25 or 50 minutes, not 30 or 60.
- No agenda = no meeting.
- Finishing early is a feature.

## Decision-making under uncertainty

As a leader, you'll often decide without complete information. That's the job.
- Name what you know and don't know.
- Consider the reversibility. Reversible decisions get made fast.
- Commit after deciding. Don't relitigate.
- Course-correct as new information arrives, publicly and without shame.

Leaders who can't decide under uncertainty become bottlenecks. Leaders who decide rashly become dangerous. Middle ground: thoughtful, committed, willing to change course on new data.

## Develop your people

The test of a leader is the level of their team five years out. Are they better than they were? Are any of them now leading teams themselves?

- Know each person's growth edge.
- Stretch assignments with support.
- Feedback on specific growth areas.
- Sponsor them (not just mentor) — open doors, name their work to others.
- Know when they should move on, even if it costs you.

The best leaders produce other leaders. That's the only real measure.

## A weekly rhythm

- 1:1 with each direct report.
- One piece of specific feedback delivered.
- One hard conversation had (not avoided).
- Protect decision-making time.
- Update up.
- Reflect on: what did I avoid this week? That's the work for next week.
