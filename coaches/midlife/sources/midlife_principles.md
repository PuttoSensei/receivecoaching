# Midlife Principles

## The middle passage

Jung's term, developed by James Hollis: the transition from the first half of life to the second. Not a crisis by necessity — a developmental threshold.

The first half of life is about building: identity, career, family, belonging. The provisional self — shaped by parents, culture, circumstance. What "they" wanted you to be.

The second half asks different questions: who am I beyond the roles? What does my deeper self want, now that I've met the expectations? What wants to be lived that hasn't yet?

Everyone reaches this threshold, if they live long enough. Not everyone crosses it consciously. Those who don't often experience later life as decline. Those who do often experience it as deepening.

## Reappraisal vs crisis

**Midlife reappraisal:** taking the unease seriously as a developmental task. Slow work. Reflection. Honest conversation with yourself and trusted others. Small experiments. Changes made thoughtfully over months or years.

**Midlife crisis:** the avoidance version. Dramatic outer change to escape the inner question. New car, affair, sudden career blow-up, disappearing from long-standing commitments. Often expensive, often regretted.

The unease behind both is the same. The response is different. Reappraisal goes toward the question; crisis runs from it.

## The unlived life

Jung: "The greatest burden a child must bear is the unlived life of its parents."

By midlife, you have probably lived several versions of a life — the student, the worker, the partner, the parent. You have also NOT lived several — the artist you suppressed, the work you avoided, the relationship you didn't have, the place you didn't move to, the version of yourself you didn't permit.

The unlived parts don't disappear. They wait. At midlife they often return, wanting recognition.

The work is not to live all of them. That would be chaos. The work is to recognise which unlived parts are truly wanting to emerge now — and integrate them — and which can be honoured as roads not taken, grieved briefly, and released.

## Grief for the roads not taken

You could have been other people, lived other lives, loved other loves, done other work. Not all of these are regrets. Some are simply the cost of having chosen anything at all.

At midlife, the narrowing of future possibility becomes visible. You will not be most of what you could have been. This produces real grief, not to be bypassed with "be grateful for what you have."

The grief is part of the passage. Feel it. Then integrate.

## Mortality enters the room

In the first half of life, death is theoretical. By midlife, it is concrete. Parents begin to age and die. Peers begin to die. Your own body changes. The calendar of remaining decades is finite and visible.

This is not morbid. It is clarifying. What matters changes when time is seen as limited. Many things that felt urgent stop being so. Some things long deferred move up the list.

The meditation: imagine yourself at 85, looking back. What will you wish you had done? Said? Risked? Not done? That list, taken seriously, reorganises the present.

## What fades; what deepens

**Often fades by midlife:**
- Hunger for status
- Desire to impress
- Chase of external validation
- Drive for speed and volume
- Concern for others' opinions
- Fear of missing out
- Competitive ambition

**Often deepens:**
- Desire for meaning over achievement
- Craft over quantity
- Real relationships over networks
- Solitude and silence
- Service to others
- Truth-telling
- Being rather than performing

This is not universal and not instant. But it is common enough that you can trust the pattern. If you notice old drives weakening, you are not losing your edge. You are being invited into a different kind of life.

## The shadow catches up

Whatever was denied, suppressed, or projected in the first half tends to return with force in the second. The relentless achiever hits burnout. The perpetual pleaser finally rages. The shut-down romantic suddenly falls in love. The "nice" person discovers their capacity for darkness.

This is not pathology. It is integration asking to happen. The parts that were set aside to build the first-half life are now knocking.

The work: own them. Don't act them out destructively (affair, blow-up career exit, massive life demolition). Do bring them into awareness. Give them some room. Integrate the anger that was never allowed, the tenderness that was considered weakness, the longing that was dismissed as impractical.

## The body changes

Menopause, andropause, metabolism shifts, recovery slows, sleep changes, hormones change, injuries take longer to heal. These are real. They affect energy, mood, libido, mental clarity.

This is not a disease. It is the body progressing. But it deserves attention:
- Medical check-ups become more important
- Strength training becomes more important (muscle and bone loss is real)
- Hormonal support where appropriate (discuss with a doctor)
- Sleep becomes more fragile and more important to protect
- Alcohol hits harder and recovers slower
- Diet matters more

Treat the body as an aging partner, not an old enemy.

## Marriage in midlife

Long marriages often hit rapids in this passage. Sometimes they need renewal — long-deferred conversations, therapy, honesty, re-falling in love with the person who also changed.

Sometimes they reach a genuine end. People grow in different directions; the container doesn't hold.

The work is discernment, not impulse. Crisis-driven exit (the affair, the sudden announcement) usually produces regret. Thoughtful, eyes-open, professionally-supported reckoning — including the possibility of deep renewal — produces either a better marriage or a cleaner ending.

Couples or individual therapy, discernment counselling specifically: strong tools here.

## The unexpected opening

Midlife is also often when:
- Creative gifts long suppressed emerge
- People start writing, painting, making
- Second careers begin (often more meaningful than the first)
- New friendships of depth arrive
- Relationships with adult children transform
- Political or spiritual life deepens
- Community of similar-age peers, doing similar work, forms

This is not consolation prize. It is the second half beginning. Many people's most alive, generative, true years are in their 50s, 60s, 70s — IF they made the passage consciously.

## What to do this season

- Read: Hollis "Finding Meaning in the Second Half of Life" (short, dense, useful). Richo "How to Be an Adult in Relationships." Frankl "Man's Search for Meaning."
- Write: the unlived lives. The grief for what won't be. The still-wants-to-be.
- Talk: to peers going through the same passage. To an elder who has crossed it. To a therapist if you have one.
- Walk: slowly. Often. Alone.
- Don't: quit the marriage, career, or life without sustained reflection.
- Do: make small experiments that test new directions without blowing up the good.

## When to seek professional support

- Severe depression lasting weeks
- Sustained suicidal thoughts
- Marriage in serious distress
- Substance use rising
- A pattern of impulsive destructive decisions
- Identity collapse that's disabling

These warrant therapy, couples counsel, or medical attention. Midlife work is not DIY for everything. It is often best done with support.

## A seasonal practice

- **Monthly:** write for an hour. What wants to be said? What is wanting to be lived? What am I avoiding?
- **Seasonally:** longer reflection. Walk, retreat, journal. What is the direction I'm facing?
- **Annually:** one honest review of the bigger questions. What would I regret not doing in the next decade? What is asking for my attention?
- **Ongoing:** keep company with people who are doing this work. Depth work in isolation is hard.

The passage is long. You are not late. You are right on time.
