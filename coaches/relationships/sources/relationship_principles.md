# Relationship & Communication Principles

## Fact vs story

Every complaint has two layers: what happened (fact) and what it meant (story).

- *Fact:* "She didn't reply for three hours."
- *Story:* "She doesn't care about me."

The story is almost never the only possible interpretation, but it feels like the only one. The first job in any conflict is to separate them. Facts are what you both agree happened. Everything else is narrative you added.

## The unspoken need

Complaints are usually needs in disguise. Behind "you never listen" is "I need to feel heard right now." Behind "you're always late" is "I need to feel like I matter to you." The complaint attacks. The need connects. Translate before you speak.

## Boundary vs demand

- *Demand:* "You can't talk to me that way."
- *Boundary:* "If you raise your voice at me, I'll step out of the room and we can talk later when it's calmer."

A demand tries to control the other person. A boundary states what you will do. Boundaries work because they are about you. Demands create resistance because they are about them.

## Hot vs cold

Do not send the message you drafted while you were hot. Write it. Save it. Sleep on it. Re-read it tomorrow. If you still mean it and it still serves the relationship, send then. Nine out of ten times you will soften it. One out of ten times you will delete it entirely.

## Repair

Repair is not apology. Apology says "I was wrong." Repair says "I see the impact, I take responsibility for my part, and here's what I'll do differently." Repair is specific. "I'm sorry you feel that way" is not repair — it is defence dressed as apology.

## When to get out

Most relationship problems are fixable with honest communication. Some are not. Red flags include: a pattern of contempt, physical violence or threat of it, coercive control, refusal to ever acknowledge any impact of their behaviour. If you recognise these, coaching on communication is not the right tool. Get professional support.
