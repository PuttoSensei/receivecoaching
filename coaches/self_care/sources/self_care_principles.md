# Self-Care Principles

## Real self-care vs cosmetic self-care

**Cosmetic self-care:** face masks, scented candles, bubble baths, retail therapy — while the underlying overwork, people-pleasing, or neglect continues.

**Real self-care:** enough sleep, eating properly, saying no, the hard conversation you've been avoiding, the medical appointment you've been putting off, leaving the job that is destroying you, setting the boundary that protects your peace.

Cosmetic self-care is fine as decoration. It fails when it becomes a substitute for the structural changes that would actually let you rest.

## The basics, before anything else

Most "I need self-care" moments dissolve when you check the basics:

- Have you slept? How many hours, how well?
- When did you last eat something that wasn't sugar or caffeine?
- Have you drunk water today?
- Have you been outside, even for ten minutes?
- Have you moved your body?
- Have you had one honest, non-transactional conversation this week?

If any of these are no, start there. No amount of advanced self-care substitutes for the basics. Most burnout is the basics ignored for months.

## Self-care is not a reward

You do not earn rest by finishing your to-do list. You do not earn food by hitting a step count. You do not earn pleasure by being productive enough.

Self-care is not a prize for productivity. It is the baseline that keeps productivity (and everything else) possible. Withhold it and the whole structure eventually fails.

## Boundaries as architecture

You cannot self-care your way through a life with no boundaries. The bath helps for an evening; without the "no" to whatever is draining you, you're back in the same state next week.

Self-care and boundaries are the same project. Boundaries are what make the rest of self-care possible.

## Rest vs collapse

- **Rest:** active, chosen, restorative. A walk, a nap, a book, a slow meal, time alone or with loved people.
- **Collapse:** forced shutdown after pushing past limits. Scrolling for hours, binging, crying uncontrollably, getting ill the minute you stop.

Rest prevents collapse. Collapse is what happens when rest has been withheld too long. Schedule rest before you collapse.

## The types of rest

You do not have one rest need — you have several. Different kinds of tired need different kinds of rest:

- **Physical rest** — sleep, stillness, lying down, a massage.
- **Mental rest** — less input, quieter environment, solitude, not deciding things.
- **Emotional rest** — time with people with whom you don't perform, or time alone.
- **Social rest** — time away from people altogether, or time only with the easy ones.
- **Sensory rest** — less noise, less light, less screen, less stimulation.
- **Creative rest** — input-heavy rest (nature, art, music, beauty) rather than output-heavy work.
- **Spiritual rest** — meaning-rich activity: ritual, contemplation, service, awe.

You may be deeply exhausted in one category and well-rested in another. Knowing which kind you need is the beginning of actually getting it.

## Self-care under caregiving, illness, or shift work

When you are caring for a parent, a child, a partner, a patient — or dealing with your own illness — standard self-care advice fails. You cannot always get eight hours of sleep, hot meals, or solitude.

What you can often do:
- Micro-rests: five minutes of quiet, once an hour.
- Short walks between duties.
- Ask for help explicitly (most people want to help but need to be told what).
- Accept that this is a season. The care of yourself in hard seasons looks smaller, not less important.

## Self-care is also saying the hard thing

The conversation with your mother you've been avoiding for two years is self-care. Leaving the dysfunctional friendship is self-care. Taking yourself to the doctor you've been putting off is self-care. Ending the relationship that drains you is self-care.

The hardest acts of self-care are usually the most unglamorous — the things a scented candle will never fix.

## A minimum weekly practice

- **Daily:** enough sleep, water, one proper meal, ten minutes outside, five minutes of stillness.
- **Weekly:** one longer rest, one honest conversation, one thing done purely because you enjoy it.
- **Monthly:** one harder check-in with yourself: what's draining me, what's nourishing me, what needs to change?

That is enough.

## Caveat

If you are exhausted, joyless, unable to enjoy things you used to, or deeply flat for more than a few weeks, that is outside the scope of self-care. See a doctor. Sustained low mood is worth proper investigation.
