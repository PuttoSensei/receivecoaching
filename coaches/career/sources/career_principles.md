# Career Principles

## What do you actually want from work?

Before any career decision, get clear on the mix you want. Most people have at least four ingredients:

1. **Money** — how much, by when, with what stability.
2. **Meaning** — does this work matter to you?
3. **Flexibility** — hours, location, autonomy.
4. **Growth** — are you learning and developing, or stagnating?

You rarely get all four at the same level simultaneously. Clarity on which two matter most at this life stage is the foundation of every career decision.

## The job problem vs the career problem vs the life problem

When work feels bad, it is usually one of three things:

- **Job problem:** this specific role, team, or manager is wrong. The fix is a different job, same field.
- **Career problem:** the whole field or function is wrong for you. The fix is a pivot.
- **Life problem:** the issue is not actually work — it is relationship, health, identity, meaning. Work is just where the pain shows up. The fix is not a new job.

Diagnose before prescribing. Otherwise you change the job and discover the problem followed you.

## The sunk cost trap

You do not owe your past career choices your future. The degree you spent four years on, the decade in an industry, the status you built — these are inputs to the next decision, not obligations that dictate it.

Ask: "If I weren't already in this, would I enter it now?" If the answer is no, that is data worth taking seriously.

## Test before you leap

Before big career changes — quitting, pivoting, starting a business, going freelance — test small first. A side project. A conversation with five people in the target field. A weekend experiment. A paid freelance gig alongside the day job.

Most confident career pivots come after small tests that revealed the direction was real. Most painful career mistakes come from big leaps with no testing, fueled by fantasy.

## The informational interview is underused

Twenty minutes on a video call with someone doing the work you're considering is one of the highest-leverage career tools. It costs nothing, takes minutes to set up, and shows you the daily reality of the job rather than the marketing version.

People are more willing than you expect. The ask: "I'm exploring [field]. You seem to do [thing] well. Could I buy you a coffee / have 20 minutes of your time to ask a few questions?" Most say yes.

Do this ten times before any major pivot.

## Network as a habit, not a campaign

The people who find good work reliably have been building quiet relationships for years. Not LinkedIn-connection-as-metric networking. Real relationships with people they've actually helped, talked to, worked alongside.

Five meaningful conversations a month, kept over a decade, is worth more than a thousand LinkedIn connections.

If you are about to need a job and have nothing built, start anyway — but know that the system compounds over time, and starting later is worse than starting earlier.

## Money is not the whole thing, and also don't undercount it

Do not take a job just because it pays more if it will destroy your health or values. Also, do not take a job paying significantly less than the market on the grounds that you're "doing it for the mission". Resentment follows.

Know your number. Know the market. Negotiate. Undercharging now sets the baseline for every job after.

## Positioning beats qualifications

Most people present themselves as a list of past roles. This is weak. Strong positioning answers: *what problem do I solve, for whom, better than most*? A resume built around that is read differently than one built around history.

If you cannot complete that sentence crisply, that's the work — not editing the resume again.

## Interviews are a conversation, not an interrogation

You are evaluating them as much as they are evaluating you. Come with questions. Listen for the answers. Notice: do the people there seem alive or checked out? Does the manager respect them? Do they describe problems honestly or in corporate-speak?

Taking a job from a position of "I need to get this to survive" leads to worse outcomes than "I'm assessing whether this is right." Cultivate the second stance even when the first is closer to true.

## When work is bad

Most jobs have bad stretches. Some jobs are structurally bad. The difference:

- **Bad stretch:** 2–3 months of hard work, a difficult colleague, a tough project. Temporary. Worth weathering.
- **Structurally bad:** you dread it for most of a year, you are disrespected, the values conflict with yours, your health is suffering, your learning has stopped. Worth leaving.

If you cannot tell which you're in, write it out. The shape becomes clearer on paper than in your head.

## Story-level beliefs to stress-test

- "I'm too old to pivot" — research the average career-change age in your target field. Usually older than you think.
- "I don't have the degree" — look at actual hiring, not posted requirements. Many fields don't check.
- "I can't afford to leave" — run the actual numbers. Three months of careful spending often buys more runway than you think.

None of this means you should jump. It means decisions should be made on reality, not on stories.

## What to do this week

Whatever career question you are sitting with:
- One 20-minute conversation with someone in the adjacent space.
- One small test of an assumption.
- One honest written answer to: "If money and status didn't matter, what would I be doing?"

Do this weekly. Direction emerges.
