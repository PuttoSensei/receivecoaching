# Goal & Success Principles

## Goals without process are wishes

A goal is a destination. A process is the road. Most people set the destination and skip the road, then wonder why they didn't arrive.

The question that matters is not "what is your goal" but "what will you do this week, precisely, to move toward it?"

## SMART, but not sacred

SMART goals — Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound — are a good floor, not a ceiling.

- **Specific:** "lose weight" fails. "Lose 5 kg" passes.
- **Measurable:** if you can't measure it, you can't know if you're winning.
- **Achievable:** stretch, but not fantasy. If the gap is too big, the brain treats it as not real.
- **Relevant:** why this goal, now? If you can't answer this crisply, the goal won't hold.
- **Time-bound:** with a deadline. Without one, it drifts forever.

## Leading vs lagging indicators

- **Lagging:** the outcome. Weight. Revenue. Book finished.
- **Leading:** the behaviour that produces the outcome. Daily workouts. Sales calls. Words written.

You cannot control lagging indicators directly — they emerge from leading ones. Most people measure only lagging, get frustrated when the number doesn't move, and give up. The fix: stop watching the scale every day. Watch the workout count.

## Consistency beats intensity

A person who runs 15 minutes every day for a year will be far fitter than one who runs 90 minutes twice then quits. The brain, the body, and compound interest all favour consistency.

Rule of thumb: cut the intensity in half and doing it twice as often. Every time.

## The minimum viable habit

The biggest mistake in habit design is starting too big. "I'll run 5 km every morning" fails on day 4 when you're tired, and then fails forever because the identity "I am someone who runs" is gone.

Start absurdly small. Two push-ups. One page. Five minutes. Small enough that skipping it feels ridiculous. Then let it grow naturally as the habit takes root. The goal of the first month is not progress — it is establishing the identity.

## Implementation intentions

Vague: "I will exercise more."
Specific: "At 7:00 am every weekday, immediately after I put on the kettle, I will do 10 minutes of stretching."

The second beats the first in every study. Name the *when*, the *where*, the *trigger*. The brain does not respond to vague intentions, but it responds strongly to rehearsed specific ones.

## Habit stacking

New habits stick when you attach them to an existing anchor.

- "After I pour my morning coffee, I will write for 15 minutes."
- "After I close my laptop at the end of the work day, I will walk for 20 minutes."
- "After I brush my teeth at night, I will stretch for 5 minutes."

The existing habit cues the new one. You don't have to remember — the trigger does the work.

## Never miss twice

You will miss days. That is not the problem. The problem is the narrative you build when you miss: "I've broken the streak, so what's the point."

The rule: missing once is inevitable. Missing twice is a pattern. After one miss, the only priority is showing up the next day, even if tiny. That's how the habit survives.

## Environment design beats willpower

If the cookies are in the cupboard, willpower is the variable. If they're not in the house, willpower is irrelevant.

Design the environment to make the good behaviour easy and the bad behaviour hard. Prep the gym clothes the night before. Remove the app. Put the book on the pillow. Friction shapes behaviour more reliably than motivation.

## The identity shift

Goals at the level of outcome are weaker than goals at the level of identity.

- Weak: "I want to lose 10 kg."
- Stronger: "I want to be healthy."
- Strongest: "I am someone who looks after their body."

Every time you take the action, you cast a vote for that identity. The more votes, the more the identity holds, and the easier the behaviour becomes.

## One goal at a time (per area)

Pick one goal per life domain at any given time. Two simultaneous goals in the same area — "lose weight and run a marathon" — usually means neither. Consecutive is fine. Simultaneous is usually not.

## Setbacks are data

When you fall off, do not moralise. Diagnose.

1. What exactly happened?
2. What condition triggered it?
3. What will you adjust to prevent the same condition tomorrow?
4. When do you restart?

Then restart. No guilt tax. No "I'll start on Monday." Today, smaller, again.

## Review weekly, adjust monthly, commit quarterly

- **Weekly:** did I do the leading indicators? What got in the way?
- **Monthly:** is the goal still right? Is the plan working?
- **Quarterly:** what am I committing to for the next 90 days?

Annual goals fail because a year is too far. 90 days is close enough to feel real, long enough to produce change.
