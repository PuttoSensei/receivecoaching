# Grief Principles

## Grief is not a problem to solve

This is the foundational misunderstanding. Grief is not a bug to fix, a condition to overcome, or a project to optimise. It is the natural response to losing someone or something that mattered.

The goal is not to get over grief. It is to live through it, learn to carry it, and eventually have a life big enough to hold both the loss and the living.

## You don't get over it. You integrate it.

An older model of grief said: work through the stages and come out the other side whole. The modern understanding: grief does not end. It changes form. You are not waiting to stop grieving; you are growing a life that includes this loss.

Most people find that the sharp-stabbing phase transforms over time into something quieter — waves that still come but with less force, space between them, the person or loss present but not all-consuming. This is integration, not resolution.

## The stages are not a staircase

Kubler-Ross's five stages — denial, anger, bargaining, depression, acceptance — were never meant to be linear. She wrote about them for the terminally ill, not the bereaved. They were descriptive, not prescriptive.

You will feel multiple at once. You will circle back. You will feel "acceptance" and then be levelled by a smell or a song. This is how grief works. You are not backsliding.

## Grief is physical

Grief lives in the body, not just the mind. Common physical symptoms:

- Deep exhaustion
- Chest heaviness, tightness
- Appetite loss or compulsive eating
- Sleep disruption — inability to sleep, or wanting to sleep all the time
- Brain fog, memory problems, difficulty concentrating
- Weakened immunity, susceptibility to colds and infections
- Aches, pains, a physical sense of heaviness
- A broken-heart-feeling that is sometimes measurable (Takotsubo cardiomyopathy is real, though rare)

These are not signs of failure or mental illness. They are grief in the body.

## Take the body seriously

Because grief depletes the body, basics matter more in grief, not less:
- Water
- Simple food, regularly, even when appetite is gone
- Sleep (or rest when sleep won't come)
- Gentle movement, sunlight, a walk
- Medical attention if something doesn't feel right

A depleted body makes grief harder. Tend to the body as part of tending to the grief.

## Protect yourself from bad grief responses

Most people are terrible at grief. They mean well and they will say things that hurt you. Common missteps from the surrounding world:

- "They're in a better place"
- "At least they didn't suffer"
- "At least you have other children / other family / your health"
- "Time heals"
- "Everything happens for a reason"
- "You should be feeling better by now"
- "I know exactly how you feel" (they don't)

Build a thicker skin, or a tighter circle, or both. You do not owe people education about your grief while you are in it.

## The small circle that can actually be with this

One of the gifts of grief is discovering who can actually sit with you in the dark. It is usually two or three people. They are not always the ones you expected.

Protect access to them. Let the others do less important things. Build the inner circle deliberately.

## Continuing bonds

Modern grief research has overturned the old "let them go" advice. Healthy grief often involves continuing bonds — an ongoing, changed relationship with the person who died.

- Talking to them
- Keeping an object, a piece of clothing
- Visiting a place they loved
- Marking their birthday, the anniversary
- Making decisions by asking what they would have wanted
- Feeling their presence

None of this is pathological. It is how many people integrate loss and continue to love across death.

## Ritual matters

Lighting a candle. Visiting their grave. Writing them a letter. Cooking their recipe. Taking their walk. These small acts are not sentimental weakness — they are the language of grief.

Design your own rituals if the existing ones don't fit. Mark the anniversaries. Let the day be different. Don't pretend it's a normal day.

## Grief for ambiguous losses

Some losses don't fit the category of death. Grief for:
- A parent with dementia (alive but gone)
- A relationship ended
- A friendship faded
- A job or career ended
- An imagined future no longer possible
- Ability lost to illness or age
- A community left behind
- A version of yourself that ended

These count. They are real grief. They often receive less recognition, less ritual, less support. Name them for what they are.

## The anniversaries and firsts

The first birthday, the first holiday, the first season turn, the first year anniversary of the loss — these hit hard. Often harder than you expect.

Plan for them. Tell your inner circle in advance. Decide in advance how you'll spend them. Don't just hope the day goes okay. It usually doesn't.

## Years later

Grief doesn't obey a schedule. Year 3, year 5, year 10 can bring waves. A song in a supermarket. A photograph you forgot existed. A stranger who looks like them. This is normal. It is love, still.

## When to seek professional support

Grief is not a diagnosis; complicated grief (or prolonged grief disorder) can be. Consider seeing a grief counsellor or therapist if:

- More than 12 months in, the grief is still completely disabling
- You are unable to function in basic daily ways
- You have persistent suicidal thoughts or plans
- You are using substances heavily to cope
- The loss was traumatic (suicide, homicide, sudden violent death, child death)
- You are stuck in a single emotion (usually guilt or rage) with no movement
- You feel numb and disconnected from yourself and others for long stretches

Grief therapy, including specific modalities for complicated grief, is effective. You do not have to do this alone.

## If you are in crisis

If you are thinking of ending your life:
- Call Samaritans (UK) 116 123
- 988 (US)
- Or your local crisis line
- Or go to A&E

The wave will pass. Please stay.

## The long slow work

Grief is not a season. It is a relationship with loss that changes over time. Year one is often the hardest. Year two is sometimes harder, because the community support fades while you are still in the middle of it. Year three onward, the integration begins to feel more reliable.

You are not broken. You are grieving. Grief is love with nowhere to go. Let it be what it is.
