# Creative Principles

## Show up

This is the whole job, reduced to two words. You can't write, paint, compose, or make anything if you don't show up to the work.

Inspiration is not the starting line. Showing up is. Inspiration usually arrives during the work, not before. Waiting to feel inspired is how most books never get written, most songs never finished, most paintings never started.

The professional shows up whether the muse is there or not. Over time, the muse learns to show up because she knows you'll be there.

## Shitty first drafts

Anne Lamott's gift. Your job is not to write a good first draft. Your job is to write a first draft. Bad, embarrassing, wrong. You cannot revise what does not exist.

Give yourself permission to be bad on paper. Truly bad. The draft is private. Nobody has to see it. You can delete every word. But write it first.

The great works of art you admire went through embarrassing early drafts. You just didn't see them.

## Daily practice beats occasional bursts

Thirty minutes a day for a year beats a heroic weekend every three months. The brain builds what you repeat.

The magic isn't in the quantity of any session. It is in showing up regularly enough that the work becomes part of who you are, not something you do when conditions are right.

## The Resistance

Steven Pressfield's name for the force that opposes creative work. It is real and it is universal. The moment you commit to doing the thing that matters, Resistance arrives:

- The dishes urgently need doing.
- You need more research before you can start.
- You're not ready yet.
- You're not good enough yet.
- Someone else has already done it better.
- This isn't a good time.

Pressfield: "Resistance is always lying and full of shit."

Name it. Notice it. Push through anyway. The Resistance will still be there tomorrow, and the day after, for every project. Your job is to act in its presence, not to wait for it to leave.

## Quota over quality

When blocked, measure volume, not merit. Count words, pages, hours, canvases, recordings, whatever. The brain can argue about quality forever and stall. It can't argue that you didn't write the 1,000 words — you either did or didn't.

Quality takes care of itself over time if volume is there. But the reverse is not true: obsessing over quality without producing volume usually leads nowhere.

Trollope wrote 2,500 words every morning before work. Paid attention to the clock, not the quality. Produced 47 novels.

For seasoned work at the top of craft, this reverses. But for blocked, beginning, or returning work, quota breaks the freeze.

## Your taste is ahead of your skill

Ira Glass's famous insight. Early in the work, you can recognise what great looks like but can't yet make it. The gap between what you want to make and what you can make is agony.

Everyone who ever made good work went through this. The only way across is volume and time. Keep making, keep trying, keep being bad until you aren't.

Most people quit in this part of the curve. The ones who don't become the good ones.

## Finishing is a separate skill from starting

Many creatives are brilliant at starting. Notebooks full of ideas, folders of opened projects, beginnings everywhere. Finishing is a muscle that builds separately.

Build it on small projects first. Finish a short story before attempting the novel. Finish a song before the album. Finish a sketch before the painting. Each finished small thing teaches the brain: completion is possible. That confidence then supports the bigger work.

If you have many started and few finished: lower the stakes of the project until you can reasonably finish it. Then finish it. Then graduate up.

## Sharing is its own terror

Finishing is hard. Sharing is harder. The vulnerability of putting your work in front of others — the dread of rejection, the dread of irrelevance (worse for some), the possibility they'll think less of you — all of this is real.

Share anyway. In small, survivable doses first. One person whose taste you trust. Then a few more. Then more public. Build the callus.

"The work is the work. Your job is to make it and send it out. How it's received is not your job." — generic paraphrase of a lot of working artists.

## The two-hour rule

Most creative people have 2–4 hours of real creative work per day. That's it. Beyond that, output declines or quality drops. This is biological, not a failure of willpower.

Protect those hours ruthlessly. Don't fill them with admin, meetings, email. Schedule the rest of life around the creative hours, not the other way around.

The rest of the day can be the rest of life — correspondence, errands, reading, exercise, family, deliberate rest. You do not need to be "on" for 8 hours. Most who try produce worse work.

## Morning pages

Julia Cameron's practice. Three pages, longhand, first thing, unedited. Stream of consciousness. Not meant to be read back.

What it does: clears the mental static so the real work can emerge. Surfaces what's underneath. Regulates the nervous system. Builds the daily habit of showing up to the page.

Not everyone needs them. Many find them transformative. Worth trying for 30 days before judging.

## The artist's date

Cameron's other big one. Once a week, take yourself somewhere novel, sensory, interesting — alone.

A museum, a flea market, a garden, a different neighbourhood, a strange shop, a concert of unfamiliar music. Alone. Not with a friend. Not with work to do.

Why: creative input matters. An artist with no input eventually has nothing to draw from. Solitary encounter with beauty, strangeness, or novelty is fuel.

## Constraints create, freedom paralyses

The blank page, total freedom, infinite choice — these kill creative work for most people. Constraints create.

- Write a poem with exactly 14 lines, iambic pentameter.
- Paint using only three colours.
- Make a film under 3 minutes.
- Write a story that takes place in one room.

Narrow the problem and the brain engages. Widen it and the brain drifts.

If you're blocked, add constraints.

## Depletion is not cured by more effort

When you are empty — no ideas, no energy, no draw to the work — pushing harder makes it worse. This is not lack of discipline. It is depletion.

The cure:
- Rest
- Input (artist's date, reading, travel, time with other makers)
- Nature
- Walking without headphones
- Sleep
- Time away from the work entirely

Filling the well is as much a part of creative practice as emptying it. Creators who only output eventually stop producing good work, then stop producing at all.

## Identity

You are what you regularly do. Action defines identity, not feeling.

Not: "I want to be a writer."
Is: "I am a writer, because I write every day, even when it's bad, even when nothing good comes of it."

Claim the identity. The work follows.

## When to seek support

If the block is persistent despite the above, if depression has set in around the work, if the creative life has become a source of shame rather than craft — consider working with a therapist, especially one who works with creatives. The block is often not creative. It is psychological, with a creative face.

## A daily practice

- 30 minutes minimum on the work. Same time if possible.
- Morning pages or some equivalent clearing practice.
- Note ideas as they come (capture system, small notebook).
- One artist's date a week.
- Sleep, walk, read.
- Don't share too early; don't hide too long.

That is enough.
