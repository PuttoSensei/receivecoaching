# Addiction Recovery Support Principles

## Scope note

This is a SUPPORT coach for people already in recovery. It does not replace medical treatment, therapy, 12-step programmes, or any primary recovery support. If you are in active addiction, in crisis, or in withdrawal, those supports are what you need — not a coach.

Withdrawal from alcohol, benzodiazepines, and some other substances can be medically dangerous. Seek medical supervision.

Crisis lines:
- SAMHSA (US): 1-800-662-HELP
- Frank (UK drugs): 0300 123 6600
- Drinkline (UK): 0300 123 1110
- Emergency: 999 / 911

## The life is the work

Early recovery is about not using. That's hard enough. But the longer work — months and years — is about building a life so good you don't want to trade it for a drink or a drug.

This includes:
- Work you can stand (sometimes a total career change)
- Relationships that support your recovery
- Daily structure that doesn't leave huge empty evening hours
- Meaning — purpose larger than just avoiding substances
- Play and joy, because a life without them is brittle
- Community that knows your story

Recovery without life-building stays white-knuckle forever. Recovery with life-building becomes the foundation of something larger.

## HALT

Hungry, Angry, Lonely, Tired.

If you're feeling shaky and something's threatening, check these four first. At least one is almost always part of it. The urge to use is often the body asking for something simpler.

- Hungry → eat
- Angry → name it, move, call someone, breathe
- Lonely → call a person, go to a meeting
- Tired → rest, sleep, stop

Many, many slips happen in HALT states. Keep them off the table.

## One day at a time

AA's famous line, applicable whether you're in AA or not.

"Will I stay sober forever?" is paralysing.
"Will I use today?" is answerable.

Zoom in. Today. The next hour. The next choice. Long-term sobriety is made of short-term sobriety, repeated.

## Connection is the opposite of addiction

Johann Hari's line. A lot of addiction research points here. The opposite of addiction is not sobriety; it is human connection.

Isolation is a relapse risk. Secretive recovery is weaker than open recovery. Being known — really known, including the messy parts — is protective.

Build:
- The inner circle who know your story
- A programme or community of others in recovery
- A sponsor, therapist, or recovery coach (not me — actual ongoing human supports)
- Healthy connections outside recovery too — the whole life, not just the sobriety

## The pink cloud and the wall

- **Pink cloud:** early recovery honeymoon. Everything feels clear. Gratitude floods. Seems easy. 30, 60, 90 days in.
- **The wall:** the pink cloud fades. Ordinary life returns with its ordinary problems, but now without the escape of using. Boring. Hard. The real work begins.

If you hit the wall, you're not failing. You're entering the middle of recovery. Double down on the basics. Meetings, sponsor, supports. This is where long-term recovery is built.

## Relapse is not the end

Most people in long-term recovery have relapsed along the way. The research is clear. This is not a goal; it is a reality.

If it happens (to you or someone you love):
- Don't catastrophise.
- Return to primary supports immediately — sponsor, therapist, programme, doctor.
- Understand what led to it. What was the chain? What was the trigger?
- Restart. Not from zero — from wherever you are.
- No guilt tax. Guilt leads back to using.

One slip is not permanent defeat. Disappearing into shame after a slip is how the slip becomes a longer relapse.

## Identity transition

Recovery is behaviour change AND identity change.

"I am not someone who drinks" is different from "I am trying not to drink." The former holds better. But it takes time to become true.

The identity is built by repeated action. Every sober day votes for the identity. Every meeting. Every conversation where you mention it. Every choice to leave the environment early.

## Meaning and purpose

People in long-term recovery who do well are almost always doing something they find meaningful. Work, service, family, craft, community, sponsoring others, building something.

Without meaning, boredom eventually threatens sobriety. The mind needs somewhere to go.

The meaning doesn't have to be grand. Raising children. Making one honest thing. Helping others in recovery. Running a small business. Mastering a craft. Teaching.

Whatever it is, have it.

## The body underneath

Recovery is physical, not just psychological.
- Sleep is often wrecked in early recovery. Repairs over months.
- Anxiety often spikes when the substance is removed.
- Cravings have somatic signatures (restlessness, agitation, salivation, racing thoughts).
- Appetite, libido, energy all often shift.
- For alcohol specifically, the liver, sleep architecture, and mood can take 6–12 months to normalise.

Care for the body. Sleep, food, water, movement, sunlight, medical checkups. A depleted body is more vulnerable to relapse.

## Relationships after recovery

- **Drinking/using friends:** usually need renegotiation. Some can stay in your life in different form. Some cannot. Grieve what needs to end.
- **Family:** often has its own recovery work. Al-Anon and similar exist for the family of addicts. Your recovery may trigger theirs.
- **Partners:** couples therapy is often useful. Recovery changes the dynamic. The partner who was the caretaker may not know how to relate to you now.
- **New relationships:** most guidance says wait at least 6–12 months of stable recovery before dating seriously. The distraction and drama of new relationships is a known relapse risk.

## Work and career

Some people can return to their old work. Some need to change it — especially if it was built around drinking culture (sales, hospitality, entertainment, some corporate environments).

The question: does this work support my sobriety, or threaten it?

If it threatens it, over time, you'll need to change either the work or your relationship to it. Usually the work.

## Triggers: know yours

Common ones:
- **HALT** states
- **Specific people** you used with
- **Places** where you used
- **Times** — evenings, Fridays, anniversaries, holidays
- **Emotions** — anger, shame, grief, boredom, even happiness
- **Celebrations** where everyone else is drinking
- **Stress spikes** — work crisis, family crisis, health crisis

Know yours. Plan for them. Don't walk blind into a predictable trigger thinking willpower will carry you.

## Warning signs (for yourself or someone you love)

- Isolation, withdrawing from supports
- Resentments building
- Grandiosity, feeling "cured"
- Stopping meetings, therapy, programme
- Glorifying past using ("it wasn't that bad")
- Keeping secrets
- HALT states being unaddressed
- Romantic or financial crisis not being processed

Any of these warrant a return to supports, a conversation with your sponsor/therapist, a renewed commitment. Don't wait until you pick up.

## Beware the external cure

- A new relationship will not cure this.
- A geographic move will not cure this.
- A new career will not cure this.
- A spiritual experience will not cure this (though it may support the work).
- Money will not cure this.

The only thing that builds long-term recovery is the daily work of recovery, built into the structure of your life. External changes without that are usually setup for relapse.

## Joy in recovery

Sobriety is not monastic suffering. The goal is a full life — pleasures, depths, highs, accomplishments, friendships, beauty, play.

If recovery feels only like loss and restriction, something is missing. Find what lights you up that isn't substance-mediated. Music, nature, dancing, cooking, craft, sport, service, travel, reading, making things, being with people you love.

The promise of recovery is not just avoiding the old life. It is a better life than the one using was helping you escape from.

## A weekly practice

- Primary recovery supports — meetings, sponsor, therapist — on schedule, non-negotiable.
- HALT check-ins daily.
- One person you told something real to this week.
- Body care basics.
- One act that built the life you're in recovery for — creative, relational, physical, meaningful.
- Warning signs check: am I isolating? Building resentment? Getting cocky?
- Gratitude, not as performance but as honest noticing of what's become possible.

This is enough. It is also a lot. You're doing real work.
