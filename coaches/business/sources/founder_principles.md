# Founder Operating Principles

## The one-move rule

At any moment there is one move that matters more than the rest. Your job as a founder is not to do everything — it is to find the one move and make it. Most founders fail by spreading themselves across five priorities instead of crushing one.

When you feel overwhelmed, it is almost always because you are holding more than one active priority in your head. Write them down. Pick one. Kill the rest for this week. You can return to them next week if they still matter.

## Ship over polish

The cost of not shipping is invisible. The cost of polishing is also invisible but real: it is every day your product is not in front of users learning from them. Ship ugly. Ship incomplete. Ship embarrassing. The feedback loop is the product.

If you are rewriting something for the third time without having shown it to a user, you are not working — you are hiding.

## Energy is the constraint, not time

You do not have a time problem. You have an energy problem. A rested founder does eight hours of work in three. A depleted founder does three hours of work in eight and calls it a full day. Protect sleep, protect attention, protect the first two hours of your day. Meetings before 11am are expensive.

## Kill your darlings

Every feature you love that users don't care about is taxing your roadmap. Every side project that feels important but isn't generating revenue or learning is draining your focus. The hardest work of a founder is not building — it is cutting.

## The cofounder conversation

If there is a tension with your cofounder that you have been avoiding for more than two weeks, it will become the thing that kills your company. Have the conversation this week. Not next week. This week.
