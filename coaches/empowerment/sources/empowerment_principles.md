# Empowerment Principles

## Victim stance vs author stance

- **Victim stance:** Life is happening TO me. The language is "they made me", "I have to", "I can't", "it's always like this".
- **Author stance:** Life is happening, and I am choosing my response. The language is "I'm choosing", "I will not", "I want", "the move I'll make is...".

Both stances are available in almost any situation. Real constraints exist — but so does the choice of stance. Empowerment is not pretending you have unlimited options. It is choosing from the ones you do have.

## Where the power leaks

Most people give their power away in predictable ways:

- **To authority figures** whose approval they still seek (parents, ex-teachers, bosses long left behind).
- **To the past** — old stories about what they can and can't do.
- **To "they"** — vague external forces that supposedly prevent action.
- **To should** — rules they did not author running their life.
- **To comparison** — measuring themselves against other people's trajectories.
- **To inertia** — "that's just how I am" as permission to stay.

Naming where the power leaks is the first step to taking it back.

## "Have to" is almost always "choose to"

Very few things you do are actually required. Most are chosen, with consequences for not doing them.

- "I have to go to this meeting" → "I am choosing to go because otherwise I will damage this working relationship."
- "I have to stay in this job" → "I am choosing to stay for now because I am not yet ready to take the income hit."

The rephrase reveals agency. You are choosing — and you can choose differently if the cost is worth paying. Or you can keep the same choice, but now own it.

## Real constraint vs learned helplessness

There are real constraints: money, time, family responsibility, disability, systemic injustice, geography, other people's power. These are not imaginary, and empowerment coaching that pretends they are is cruel.

But there is also learned helplessness — the conviction that nothing can change, absorbed from past environments where nothing could. Much of what feels like real constraint is in fact this old learning misapplied to new circumstances.

The discernment work: what is actually blocking me here, and what am I believing is blocking me out of habit?

## Small promises kept

The foundation of felt empowerment is keeping small promises to yourself. You said you would walk. You walked. You said you would send the email. You sent it.

Every kept promise casts a vote for "I am someone who follows through." Enough votes, and agency becomes a felt fact, not an aspiration.

Start tiny. A glass of water. A 5-minute walk. A single page. Boring on purpose. Keep them. The boring ones build the foundation everything else stands on.

## Reclaim through refusal

Saying no is an act of authorship. Every "no" that was needed and not said is a piece of your power left on the floor. Every clear "no" — without 500 words of justification — is a piece reclaimed.

Practise saying no to small things first. The restaurant you don't want. The invitation you don't want. The favour you don't have the energy for. Build the tolerance. Use it on the bigger ones when they come.

## Empowerment is relational, not just individual

The empowered move is sometimes doing less, asking for help, leaving a toxic situation, joining with others, or letting yourself be cared for. Individualist empowerment — white-knuckled self-reliance — is a trap.

The question is not always "how do I do this alone". It is sometimes "who do I need in my corner, and what would it cost me to ask?"

## What you do not choose vs what you are choosing now

You did not choose: your birth family, your early circumstances, the events done to you, other people's behaviour, much of your wiring.

You are choosing: how you respond, what you do with what you've been handed, what you make of the next hour.

This distinction matters. Empowerment is not taking responsibility for what was done to you. It is taking authorship of what you do next.

## The weekly audit

Once a week, ask:
- Where did I give my power away this week? To whom? In what form?
- What did I author consciously? What am I proud of?
- What is the next act of authorship I will take?

That's the practice.
