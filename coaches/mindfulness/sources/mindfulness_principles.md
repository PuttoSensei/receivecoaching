# Mindfulness Principles

## What mindfulness is

Mindfulness is paying attention, on purpose, in the present moment, without judgement. That's Kabat-Zinn's working definition and it holds up.

It is not: clearing your mind, achieving calm, thinking positive thoughts, escaping. It is noticing whatever is actually here — pleasant, unpleasant, neutral — with an attitude of curiosity and without needing it to be different.

## The three anchors

When attention scatters, you return to one of three anchors:

1. **Breath** — the most portable. You always have it. Notice the next inhale. Notice the exhale.
2. **Body** — sensation in the hands, the feet, the seat. Where is contact? What is the temperature?
3. **Sound** — the ambient soundscape, without labelling or chasing.

You do not have to feel calm. You only have to return. The returning is the practice.

## Simple is not easy

The instructions are famously simple: sit, breathe, notice, return. The practice is famously difficult. The mind wanders a hundred times in ten minutes. That is not failure. That is the mind.

A ten-minute meditation in which you noticed you were distracted a hundred times is a hundred reps of coming back. That is exactly the training.

## Formal vs informal practice

- **Formal:** dedicated sitting. Five to twenty minutes, most days. Timer on. Same place ideally.
- **Informal:** mindfulness in ordinary moments — eating the first bite of dinner attentively, walking from the car to the door, one breath before a difficult email.

Informal practice is where the change shows up. The cushion trains the capacity; ordinary life is where it lands.

## The STOP practice

Four steps, 30 seconds, doable anywhere:

- **S** — Stop. Whatever you are doing, pause.
- **T** — Take a breath. One conscious breath.
- **O** — Observe. Body, thought, feeling, surroundings. No fixing.
- **P** — Proceed. Chosen rather than automatic.

Do this ten times a day and your life changes.

## Response vs reaction

Mindfulness creates a gap between stimulus and response. In that gap lives choice. Without the gap, you react automatically — old patterns, old wiring, old hurts. With it, you can respond as the person you want to be.

The practice is literally the practice of widening that gap, one noticing at a time.

## Non-judgement

The hardest instruction. You will judge. You will judge your judging. The practice is not to stop judging — it is to notice judgement arising and let it pass without feeding it.

When you notice "this is boring" or "I'm bad at this" — that is itself mindfulness. The noticing is the practice. Return to the breath.

## Emotions in the body

Mindfulness teaches you to feel emotions as bodily sensation before story. Anger is heat in the chest and jaw. Fear is cold in the belly. Sadness is heaviness in the throat.

Sit with the sensation for thirty seconds without going into the story. It almost always moves. Emotions that move do not get stuck.

## What mindfulness is not for

- It is not a productivity hack. Treating it that way makes it brittle.
- It is not always helpful for unresolved trauma. Silent eyes-closed meditation can destabilise some people. Trauma-sensitive approaches use eyes open, short practices, grounding in the body and environment first. If sitting feels worse, stop and find a trauma-informed teacher.
- It is not a substitute for therapy, medication, or practical action.

## A simple daily practice

- Set a timer for 10 minutes.
- Sit upright, somewhere comfortable.
- Close or lower your eyes.
- Find the breath. Follow it. When you drift, return. That's it.
- End when the timer goes.

Then, three times during the day: STOP. One conscious breath. Notice. Proceed.

That's enough. More is optional.
