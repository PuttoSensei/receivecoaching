# Sleep Principles

## Scope note

This is behavioural and lifestyle guidance. It is not a substitute for medical assessment of sleep disorders. If you suspect sleep apnea, narcolepsy, restless legs, or have chronic insomnia lasting 3+ months, see a doctor. For chronic insomnia, CBT-I (Cognitive Behavioural Therapy for Insomnia) is the first-line evidence-based treatment — not sleep hygiene advice alone.

## Why sleep matters

Sleep is not downtime. It is active biological maintenance:
- Memory consolidation
- Emotional regulation
- Immune function
- Metabolic regulation
- Glymphatic clearing of brain waste (including amyloid plaques)
- Hormone regulation
- Physical recovery

A single night of poor sleep affects mood, decision-making, appetite regulation, immune response, and performance. Chronic sleep deprivation correlates with obesity, cardiovascular disease, diabetes, depression, cognitive decline, and shorter lifespan.

You cannot "catch up" fully on weekends. Regular, adequate sleep is not a luxury. It is a biological requirement.

## The three levers

Of the many sleep inputs, three dominate:

1. **Wake time consistency** — more than bedtime. The body sets its rhythm by when you wake.
2. **Morning light exposure** — outdoor, ideally in first hour after waking. The single most powerful circadian input.
3. **Evening light dimming** — lower lights, fewer bright screens in the last 2–3 hours. Brain uses this to anticipate sleep.

Get these three right and most sleep problems shift dramatically.

## Consistency beats duration for many people

A consistent 7-hour sleeper often functions better than an inconsistent 8-hour sleeper. The body predicts when sleep comes and makes the most of the window.

Irregular schedules — especially weekend "catch up" sleeping late — create permanent mini-jetlag. Aim for consistency, especially wake time. Allow one hour of drift on weekends maximum.

## Morning light, every day

10–30 minutes of outdoor daylight within the first hour of waking. Even cloudy daylight is 10x brighter than indoor lighting and does the work.

Why: morning light suppresses melatonin, sets the circadian clock, and builds pressure for evening sleepiness. Skipping morning light is a common hidden cause of evening insomnia.

In winter / dark mornings: a 10,000-lux light therapy lamp can substitute, used for 20–30 minutes in the first hour.

## Caffeine has a long half-life

Caffeine half-life is roughly 5–7 hours. That afternoon coffee is still in your system at midnight, affecting sleep architecture even if you fell asleep.

Rules of thumb:
- Cut-off at noon for most adults.
- Cut-off at 2pm maximum for the less caffeine-sensitive.
- Assess honestly: how much are you actually drinking? What time is the last one?
- Some people are slow metabolisers (genetic variation). If coffee at 10am disrupts your sleep, that's you.

## Alcohol disrupts sleep even when it helps you fall asleep

Common misunderstanding: "alcohol helps me sleep." It helps you fall asleep. It then fragments sleep, reduces REM, reduces deep sleep, and often wakes you in the early hours.

Even one drink reduces sleep quality. Several drinks reliably produce poor sleep. If you drink routinely, stopping often improves sleep markedly within a week.

## The wind-down

Brains don't flip a switch. Going from stimulating activity directly to bed doesn't work for most people.

A 30–60 minute wind-down:
- Dim lights (warm, lower intensity)
- Lower-stimulation activity (reading, light conversation, simple tasks)
- No intense screens (especially news, games, work)
- Same rough sequence each night — the routine itself cues sleep
- Bathroom, brushing teeth, pyjamas as the signal
- Optional: warm shower or bath 1–2 hours before bed (the subsequent body temperature drop aids sleep)

The consistency of the routine matters more than the specific components.

## The bedroom environment

- **Dark.** Blackout curtains or eye mask.
- **Cool.** 16–19°C / 60–67°F for most adults. Cooler than people expect.
- **Quiet.** Earplugs or white noise if your environment isn't.
- **No phone.** This is the single biggest change many people can make. Phone charges in another room. Old-fashioned alarm clock if needed.
- **Bed is for sleep (and sex).** Not work, not TV, not scrolling. The brain builds an association between bed and whatever you do there.

## If you can't fall asleep

Do not lie in bed staring at the ceiling for hours. This builds a "bed = frustrated wakefulness" association that makes future insomnia worse.

The 20-minute rule: if you've been awake 20 minutes, get out of bed. Go to another dimly lit room. Do something quiet and low-stimulation (read, something boring). Return to bed when you feel sleepy.

Don't check the time. Knowing it's 3am fuels anxiety. Turn the clock away.

## Middle-of-night waking

Common and usually not a problem unless it becomes chronic. If you wake and fall back asleep within 15–20 minutes, not an issue.

If you wake and can't return to sleep:
- Don't check the phone.
- Don't calculate how many hours you have left.
- Get up briefly if needed (20-minute rule).
- The body often settles more easily when you stop trying.

## Sleep anxiety makes sleep worse

Worrying about sleep is a reliable way to make it worse. Tracking your sleep obsessively can also make sleep worse (orthosomnia — the paradox of sleep trackers making anxious sleepers more anxious).

One bad night is fine. Humans cope. Most people have occasional bad nights and are functional the next day. Catastrophising amplifies the problem.

If you have insomnia: CBT-I specifically addresses the anxiety loops that maintain insomnia. Highly effective. Find a CBT-I therapist or use a validated app (CBT-I Coach, Sleepio, Somryst).

## Naps

- **Short nap (10–20 min):** usually beneficial, doesn't affect night sleep for most people.
- **Long nap (60–90 min):** full cycle, can feel great if not too late in the day.
- **Anything in between:** often worst — wake in the middle of a cycle, grogginess.
- **Nap before 3pm.** Later naps affect night sleep.

## Shift work

Shift work is genuinely hard on the body. The evidence is clear: chronic shift work correlates with multiple health issues. If you can avoid it, do; if you can't:
- Anchor sleep blocks of 4+ hours when possible.
- Darkness / eye mask / blackout during day sleep.
- Strategic short naps before shifts.
- Protect the sleep you can get.
- Accept the trade-off; optimise within it.

## New parents

The worst sleep deprivation of a lifetime, for 1–3 years. Standard advice fails. Realistic tools:
- Sleep when the baby sleeps, even 20 minutes.
- Trade shifts if partnered.
- Protect ONE block of consolidated sleep per parent per night if possible.
- Lower standards on everything else.
- Ask for help. Accept help. This is survival.
- It passes.

## Jet lag

- Use daytime outdoor light at the destination to reset.
- Caffeine in morning of new timezone, not later.
- Melatonin in low doses (0.3–1mg, not high doses) can help shift circadian rhythm. Talk to a pharmacist or doctor about whether and how.
- Stay hydrated.
- Go outside. Don't hide in the hotel.

## When to see a doctor

- Snoring loudly, gasping, partner reports breathing pauses (sleep apnea)
- Falling asleep at inappropriate times (lunch, driving) despite adequate night sleep
- Moving legs uncomfortably at night (restless legs syndrome)
- Acting out dreams (REM behaviour disorder)
- Chronic insomnia for 3+ months
- Daytime function significantly impaired by sleep

All of these have treatments. None are things to just push through.

## A weekly baseline

- Consistent wake time (7 days).
- 7–9 hours in bed most nights.
- Morning outdoor light most days.
- Caffeine before noon.
- No phone in bedroom.
- Wind-down routine kept.
- Alcohol minimised (or eliminated).

Do these for two weeks and most non-clinical sleep problems improve substantially.
