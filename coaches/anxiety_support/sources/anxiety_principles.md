# Anxiety Support Principles

## Scope note

This is a SUPPORT coach for everyday anxiety, stress, and worry. It is not treatment for anxiety disorders. For panic disorder, GAD at clinical severity, OCD, PTSD, social anxiety disorder, or agoraphobia, evidence-based treatments (CBT, exposure therapy, ACT, medication) administered by qualified clinicians are what's needed. This coach can support between-session life; it doesn't replace therapy.

If you have suspected clinical anxiety, see a doctor or therapist. If you are in crisis, contact: Samaritans (UK) 116 123, 988 (US), or your local crisis line.

## Anxiety is a nervous system state, not a character flaw

Anxiety is the body's alarm system — useful in moderation, disabling when stuck on. Most humans experience it. The question is proportionality and function, not presence or absence.

Some anxiety is genuinely useful: alertness, anticipation, preparation, motivation. Some is disabling: interrupts sleep, narrows your life, distorts decisions, costs you energy. Coaching works with the disabling kind without trying to eliminate anxiety entirely.

## The goal is not to feel no anxiety

The goal is to handle anxiety without being run by it. To function, decide, connect, and live a full life regardless of whether anxiety is present in any given moment.

Trying to eliminate anxiety entirely is itself an anxiety-maintaining pattern. The more you fight it, the more space it takes. The more you allow it (while not obeying it), the more it settles over time.

## Somatic tools first

When anxiety is active, cognitive work is weak. The body is in fight-flight; the thinking brain is offline. You cannot reason your way out of a dysregulated nervous system. You have to work with the body first.

Three reliable tools:

**Extended exhale breathing.** The exhale activates the parasympathetic nervous system. Longer out-breath than in-breath for 1–3 minutes.
- Box breath: inhale 4, hold 4, exhale 4, hold 4.
- 4-7-8: inhale 4, hold 7, exhale 8.

**5-4-3-2-1 grounding.** Name 5 things you can see, 4 you can hear, 3 you can touch, 2 you can smell, 1 you can taste. Pulls attention out of the head and into the senses.

**Cold exposure.** Cold water on face, cold shower, ice pack on the chest. Triggers the mammalian dive reflex, which rapidly lowers heart rate. Useful for panic-level spikes.

Do one of these BEFORE trying to think about what's anxious-making.

## Anxiety lives in the body

Not in thought. The racing mind is downstream of the activated body. If you ask "where is the anxiety?" most people can point to a specific location:
- Chest tightness
- Throat constriction
- Stomach flutter or dread
- Shoulders locked
- Jaw clenched
- Hands shaky or cold
- Heart pounding

Locating the anxiety physically, without needing to change it, often begins the settling. You're acknowledging it's there, instead of fighting a thought war.

## Name and narrate

Labelling emotions reduces their physiological intensity (Lieberman et al. research).

"I'm noticing anxiety. It's in my chest. It's about the meeting tomorrow. It's uncomfortable. I'm okay."

The last phrase matters. "I'm okay" doesn't mean the feeling isn't there. It means you're still here, still you, still functional. The feeling is a visitor, not a captor.

## Reassurance-seeking is a trap

When anxious, there's an urge to ask someone — a partner, a friend, Google, a chatbot — "am I okay? is this dangerous? what if X? are you sure?"

Reassurance works briefly. The anxiety drops for ten minutes. Then it comes back, bigger. Because the brain learned: "I can't feel okay without reassurance." The pattern strengthens.

Break the pattern gently:
- Reassure yourself briefly once, not seven times.
- Tolerate the uncertainty for longer than usual.
- Don't let the urge to check/ask be the boss.
- Notice: the worry usually doesn't come true. The prediction was wrong.

## Avoidance is a trap

Avoiding what makes you anxious reduces anxiety short-term and massively increases it long-term. The brain learns: "this must have been dangerous, because I got away from it."

- You avoid the social event → the next one is harder.
- You avoid the difficult email → the fear of it grows.
- You avoid the health check → the health anxiety compounds.

The cure is graded exposure. Not forced, not overwhelming. A ladder of small approaches, each one slightly harder than the last, each one survivable.

Exposure works because the brain updates: "I did the thing, I was uncomfortable, I was okay, the catastrophe didn't happen." Enough data points and the threat recalibrates.

## Thoughts are not commands

An anxious thought feels urgent and true. Often it is neither. It is a thought — a mental event, not a command.

- "What if something terrible happens" is a thought. It does not require action.
- "I can't handle this" is a thought. It is usually inaccurate.
- "Everyone will think I'm stupid" is a thought. It is rarely accurate.

You can notice the thought, label it ("that's an anxious thought"), and not act on it. Mindfulness practice strengthens this capacity dramatically.

## What's in my control

Anxiety narrows attention onto uncontrollable outcomes. You worry about other people's reactions, the future, possibilities.

Redirect attention deliberately:
- What can I do in the next hour?
- What is actually mine to handle?
- What's happening right now, not what might happen?

Bring attention back to this moment, this choice, this action. Anxiety loosens when you engage with the concrete present rather than the hypothetical future.

## Lifestyle factors

Baseline anxiety is hugely affected by:

**Sleep.** Under-slept people are more anxious. Full stop.

**Caffeine.** Dose-dependent anxiety booster. Many chronically anxious people are over-caffeinated. Try a week at half.

**Alcohol.** Helps acute anxiety short-term; increases baseline anxiety over days. The morning-after anxiety after drinking is real.

**Exercise.** One of the most evidence-supported anxiety reducers. Regular moderate exercise reduces baseline anxiety substantially.

**News/social media.** 24-hour alarm loops feed anxiety. Many anxious people feel markedly better with reduced intake.

**Sugar and refined carbs.** Blood sugar crashes mimic and amplify anxiety.

**Phone hygiene.** Constant input, constant alarm. Reducing phone use reduces anxiety for many.

Fix these before, or alongside, any other intervention.

## The worry window

For chronic worriers: schedule 15 minutes a day as "worry time." When worries pop up outside that window, note them for later and return to the present. Don't suppress — delay.

Most worries, when they get to the worry window, have already dissolved. The structure teaches the brain that you can acknowledge the worry without obeying it immediately.

## "Is this useful anxiety?"

Two types:

**Useful:** something actionable is producing it. The action reduces the anxiety.
- "I'm anxious about the presentation" → prepare better, rehearse, sleep. Anxiety reduces.

**Useless:** no action will reduce it. It is cycling for its own sake.
- "I'm anxious that someone might crash into me while driving tomorrow" → no action short of never driving helps. Let it pass.

Ask: "Is there something I can do about this right now?" If yes, do the smallest next thing. If no, name that, and redirect attention. The brain learns to distinguish.

## What to do in a panic spike

(Not a panic disorder treatment — for that, see a clinician.)

For an acute surge of anxiety:

1. **Name it.** "This is a panic wave. It will pass. It always passes."
2. **Slow the breath.** Long exhales.
3. **Cool the body.** Cold water on the face, hands in cold water.
4. **Ground.** 5-4-3-2-1.
5. **Move.** Walk if you can.
6. **Ride it.** It will peak at about 10 minutes and subside. It always does.

Don't fight the wave. Ride it with the body calming practices.

## When to escalate

See a doctor or therapist if:
- Anxiety is disrupting sleep, work, relationships, or daily function for more than 2–3 weeks
- You have recurring panic attacks
- Avoidance is narrowing your life
- Intrusive thoughts are distressing or obsessive in pattern
- You've had trauma (recent or historical) affecting the anxiety
- Substances are rising to cope
- Suicidal thoughts appear

Clinical-level anxiety has effective treatments (therapy, medication, CBT, exposure therapy, ACT). The barrier is usually stigma and inertia, not effectiveness. Getting help is a strength.

## A daily practice

- One anxiety-reducing body practice (breath, walk, cold, movement) at least once.
- Morning: caffeine cap, phone later, outside briefly.
- When anxious: name, locate in body, breathe out longer than in.
- Don't over-check, over-reassure, over-avoid.
- Honest self-talk: "is this useful anxiety? no? let it pass."
- Sleep, move, limit stimulants.

This is enough for most everyday anxiety. For clinical anxiety, add therapy.
