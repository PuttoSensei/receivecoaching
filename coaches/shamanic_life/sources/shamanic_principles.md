# Shamanic-Influenced Coaching — Principles & Practice

## A note of respect

Shamanism, properly understood, is not one practice. It is a name Western scholars gave to a cluster of related traditions across many indigenous cultures — Siberian, Amazonian, Andean, African, Sami, North American, and others. Each is deeply specific: to a people, a land, a language, a lineage, and an initiatory path.

What follows is not shamanism in that sense. It is coaching influenced by cross-cultural shamanic practices that have entered Western self-development, often via Michael Harner's "core shamanism" and related lineages. It is a simplified, non-initiatory subset of a much richer world. Use it with that knowledge.

If you are drawn to a specific tradition, seek teachers from within that tradition. This coaching does not substitute for that.

## The imaginal

The imaginal is not imagination in the everyday sense of "making stuff up". It is a real faculty of mind that works through image, symbol, metaphor, and dream. It is how much of the psyche speaks when words are too slow.

Shamanic-influenced practice takes the imaginal seriously as a source of knowing. When you close your eyes and a landscape appears, an animal shows up, a figure comes to speak — this is not random. It is a language. Learning to listen to it is a capacity worth developing.

You do not need to believe the figures are metaphysically real. You also do not need to believe they aren't. What matters is working with them with respect and attention.

## Journeying

A journey is a guided inner traveling, usually accompanied by rhythm — a drum, a rattle, the breath — into the imaginal landscape. The rhythm occupies the surface mind and lets deeper layers open.

A basic journey structure:

1. **Arrival.** Sit or lie down. Breath. Let the body settle. Set an intention — a question, a seeking, a prayer.
2. **Entry.** Imagine a place on the earth you love. From there, find a way down into the earth — a cave, a tree root, a well. Go down.
3. **Meeting.** Let what is there appear. An animal. A being. A landscape. Do not push. Receive what comes.
4. **Dialogue.** Ask your question. Listen. What appears — in image, feeling, or words — is the answer, even if you don't understand it yet.
5. **Return.** Thank what came. Return the way you came. Come back into the body. Ground.
6. **Integration.** Write what happened. Draw it. Sit with it. Let meaning emerge over hours or days, not in the moment.

Ten to twenty minutes. No special equipment needed. A steady drumbeat recording helps.

## The three worlds

A working map from cross-cultural shamanism:

- **Lower world:** body, instinct, earth, ancestor, root. Reached by going down — through caves, roots, waters. The world of animal allies and ancestral wisdom. For questions about instinct, healing, foundation, trauma.
- **Middle world:** ordinary reality seen with non-ordinary sight. The world of nature spirits, the living land, the place where ancestors and the living overlap. For questions about this life, this land, these relationships.
- **Upper world:** vision, teachers, larger pattern, the realm of sky and light. Reached by going up — a tree, a ladder, a cloud. For questions about direction, purpose, larger meaning.

Different questions call for different worlds. You will learn by practice which is right for which.

## Power animals and allies

In shamanic-influenced work, archetypal figures often appear as animals — bear, wolf, eagle, serpent, owl, deer. These are not pets or mascots. They are aspects of consciousness offering themselves as allies.

- The bear comes when you need strength, containment, winter.
- The eagle comes when you need vision, perspective, altitude.
- The serpent comes when you need to shed, transform, renew.
- The deer comes when you need gentleness, vigilance, softness with power.

These are suggestions, not rules. What comes to you, in your particular life, is the thing. Trust what shows up over what the book said.

Build a relationship with what appears. Learn its ways. Carry a token (a feather, a stone, a carved figure) to remind you. Let it inform how you move through the world.

## The four directions and elements

Different traditions map these differently. One usable version:

- **East — air — dawn — mind — new beginning.**
- **South — fire — midday — will — transformation.**
- **West — water — dusk — emotion — release.**
- **North — earth — night — body — wisdom.**

You can use this as a check on balance. Am I too much in my head (air)? Am I burning too hot (fire)? Am I flooded (water)? Am I stuck in inertia (earth)? Which direction am I avoiding?

Or you can work with one element deliberately for a week. Earth this week — time on the ground, slow food, carrying weight. Water next week — baths, tears if they come, the sea.

## Nature as teacher

The most reliable spirit guide you have access to is the living world itself. A tree, a river, a mountain, a particular patch of woods behind your house. Spend time with it. Sit with it. Say hello. Ask questions. Listen.

This is not romantic. It is a discipline. A sit spot — one place you return to regularly, watch in all weathers, learn over seasons — is a classic practice in many traditions and the single best way to learn from the land.

Twenty minutes, three times a week, in one place. For a year. You will know things you did not know. Not through words. Through something else.

## Ceremony

Ceremony is intentional action that marks a transition. It does not require special gear, costume, or lineage. It requires intention, attention, and witness — even if the only witness is the land, the fire, or your own attention.

Simple ceremonies:

- **Release:** write what you are done with. Burn the paper outside. Let the ash go with wind.
- **Commitment:** make the commitment aloud, to a tree, to the sky, to a candle flame. Let the commitment be witnessed.
- **Transition:** for a birthday, a new chapter, an ending — take a walk that names what has been and what is coming, mark a threshold you cross, leave an offering behind, arrive somewhere new.
- **Welcome:** when something new begins — a home, a job, a child, a project — bring it offering. Food, a song, a drop of water. Acknowledge the arrival.

The form matters less than the fact. Ceremony done simply, with intention, with witness, does real work in the psyche.

## Reciprocity

The core ethic of most indigenous traditions: everything is in relationship, and relationship requires giving as well as receiving.

You have spent your life in a culture that teaches extraction — take from the land, take from others, take from the future. Shamanic-influenced practice asks you to invert this.

- Offer water to a tree you love.
- Leave food or tobacco at a site you visit.
- Thank the animal whose life becomes your meal.
- Pick up rubbish from the place you walked.
- Offer time, attention, care to things that cannot thank you.

None of this is required to make the inner work function. All of it makes the inner work real. A spiritual practice that does not change how you treat the land and the other-than-human is incomplete.

## Soul retrieval — a coaching version

Sometimes pieces of us get left behind. In old relationships that broke badly. In traumas we could not integrate. In chapters we ended hastily. Parts of our aliveness, creativity, or capacity for love can feel missing.

A coaching version of soul retrieval:

1. Name a chapter or event where you feel something got lost.
2. In a journey or quiet reflection, return to that moment in imagination.
3. Find the part of you that was left there. Meet it. Notice its age, its state, what it needs.
4. Invite it home. Ask its permission. Bring it back with you.
5. Make an offering to that part in ordinary life — a practice, a kindness, something it was denied.

This is not therapy. For deep trauma, do this work with a qualified therapist. But for lighter losses of self, this gentle practice can restore what has drifted.

## The return

Every journey, every ceremony, every vision is worth little if it does not return to ordinary life. The measure of this work is not the vividness of the inner experience. It is how it changes Tuesday morning.

- Did the journey produce one concrete act this week?
- Did the ceremony shift one relationship?
- Did the sit spot teach you something you applied?

If yes, the work is real. If no, the work is decoration. Always end by asking: what now, in ordinary life, am I doing differently?

## Honest caveats

- This is not a substitute for medical care. If you are ill, see a doctor.
- This is not a substitute for psychotherapy. If you are in crisis, dealing with trauma, or struggling with mental illness, see a therapist.
- Plant medicines are not used in this coaching. They are powerful, they are medicine, they belong with trained practitioners in appropriate settings.
- Beware of practitioners (including yourself) who claim to see your spirit animal, diagnose your soul, or do healing on you from a distance. The authority lives in your own experience.
- Respect the traditions this work draws from. Do not take specific songs, ceremonies, or sacred items from living indigenous cultures and use them as your own.
