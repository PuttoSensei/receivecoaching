# Money Principles

## Scope note

This is general financial education, not personalised advice. For investments, taxes, major financial decisions, or specific products, consult a qualified fee-only fiduciary financial planner, accountant, or tax professional. For debt crisis, consult a non-profit credit counsellor.

## Know your numbers

The single most important move in personal finance: look at your actual numbers. Most people avoid this. The avoidance is the problem, not any particular number.

Monthly, minimum:
- **Income.** All sources. After tax.
- **Spending.** By category. What actually left your account.
- **Savings.** What you have in easily-accessible savings.
- **Debt.** What you owe, to whom, at what interest rate.
- **Net worth.** Total assets minus total debt.

This takes one hour a month. It changes everything. Most financial anxiety is fuelled by not knowing. Knowing is usually less terrifying than imagining.

## Pay yourself first

The oldest piece of personal finance wisdom that actually works. When money comes in, route a portion to savings BEFORE you spend on anything else. Even a small amount.

Automate it. Make it happen before you see the money. You cannot spend what isn't in your checking account.

Start with whatever you can — $20, £50 — and increase it when you can. The habit is the point. The amount grows.

## The emergency fund

Before anything else — before investing, before paying off low-interest debt aggressively, before any other financial goal — build an emergency fund.

- **Starter:** £1,000 / $1,000 in a savings account. First milestone.
- **Full:** 3–6 months of essential expenses in an easily-accessible savings account.

This single thing is the most de-stressing financial move most people can make. It buys you the ability to handle life's surprises without a crisis.

## Spend less than you earn

Everything in personal finance reduces to this. If you spend less than you earn, you build wealth over time. If you spend as much as or more than you earn, you do not.

The gap between income and spending is the real driver of long-term financial health. Not income alone. Not cutting lattes. The gap.

This can be widened by earning more or spending less. Both are levers. Most people focus on one and ignore the other.

## Debt: the two main strategies

If you have consumer debt (credit cards, personal loans, payday loans), two main methods work:

### The snowball (psychological)
Pay minimums on everything. Put every extra pound/dollar at your smallest balance until it's gone. Then roll that payment onto the next smallest. Psychological wins come fast, which helps consistency.

### The avalanche (mathematical)
Pay minimums on everything. Put every extra pound/dollar at the highest-interest debt. Saves the most money. Slower psychological wins.

Either works. The best one is the one you'll stick with. If you've quit debt payoff plans before, the snowball may fit better.

### Do not do
- New credit card balances while paying down old ones
- Payday loans (extortionate rates, designed to trap)
- Debt consolidation products with hidden fees or that extend your repayment forever
- Borrowing from retirement accounts except in true emergencies

### Do consider
- Balance transfers to 0% cards (strategic, if you'll pay off before the promo ends)
- Calling the creditor directly to negotiate interest rate (often works)
- Non-profit credit counselling (StepChange, National Debt Line in UK; NFCC in US)

## Lifestyle creep

As income rises, spending tends to rise with it. A modest lifestyle on £30k and £300k can both feel tight because the spending has grown to match.

The protection: when income goes up, save or invest a significant portion of the raise before adjusting spending. Build the income-spending gap wider, not keep it the same.

## Compound interest

The single most important math concept for long-term wealth. Money invested grows; the growth also grows; the growth of the growth also grows.

- £500/month invested from age 25, earning ~7% real return, reaches roughly £1m by 65.
- Same amount started at 35 reaches roughly £500k by 65.
- Started at 45, roughly £250k.

Time in the market beats timing the market. Starting early beats investing more later.

## Investing, simply

(General education only. For specifics, see a qualified advisor.)

For most people, the best long-term investing approach is:
1. **Tax-advantaged accounts first** (ISAs and pensions in UK; 401k, IRA in US; equivalents elsewhere). Especially any employer match — that's free money.
2. **Low-cost broad-market index funds.** The evidence is extensive: these beat the majority of active fund managers over long time horizons.
3. **Long time horizon.** Don't check daily. Don't panic-sell in downturns. Keep buying through cycles.
4. **Diversification.** Don't concentrate in one stock, one sector, one country.

Avoid:
- Stock picking
- Market timing
- High-fee active funds
- Speculative assets you don't understand
- Anything that promises high returns with low risk (doesn't exist)

## Money and emotion

Most money problems are emotional before they are mathematical.

Common patterns:
- **Money avoidance:** never looking at accounts. Bills unopened. Shame spiralling.
- **Money worship:** more is always better. Status through spending. Identity tied to net worth.
- **Money vigilance:** so frugal you can't enjoy what you have.
- **Underearning:** persistent undercharging, underasking, underwork as a pattern not a circumstance.
- **Inherited scarcity:** beliefs about money from the family of origin running your adult life.
- **Partner conflict:** opposite money patterns in a couple (saver/spender) without honest negotiation.

Healing these often matters more than any spreadsheet optimisation.

## Your money story

Everyone has a money story, absorbed from family, culture, early experience. It is usually below awareness and running your adult decisions.

Spend an hour writing it:
- What did your parents say about money? What did they do?
- What was the emotional tone of money conversations in your home?
- What did you inherit as belief about money, work, wealth, poverty?
- Which of these still serve you? Which don't?

This one exercise has more impact than most people expect.

## The four uses

Healthy money life includes all four:

1. **Giving** — to people, causes, community. Not guilt-driven; chosen.
2. **Saving** — emergency fund, sinking funds, future goals.
3. **Investing** — long-term, compounding, for later decades.
4. **Spending** — on what actually matters to you. Not what others think should matter.

The balance between these changes by life stage. But all four present, at any level of income, is a sign of a healthy relationship with money.

## When to get a real advisor

Consider a qualified financial planner (preferably fee-only fiduciary) when:
- Income is complex (multiple sources, self-employed, investments)
- A major life event (marriage, divorce, inheritance, house purchase, business sale)
- Retirement planning in earnest
- Estate planning
- Tax planning beyond basics
- You're genuinely stuck despite decent income

A good advisor pays for themselves. A bad one costs you more than the fees. Interview carefully. Fee-only is important — commission-based advisors have structural conflicts of interest.

## A monthly routine

- **End of month:** 30–60 min, look at numbers. Income, spending (by category), savings, debt, net worth. Write them down.
- **Mid-month:** 10-min check-in. Anything off-track?
- **Quarterly:** bigger review. Goals. Direction.
- **Annually:** full review. Insurance, beneficiaries, wills, investments, tax position.

Most people avoid this. The avoidance is the problem. The practice is the solution.
