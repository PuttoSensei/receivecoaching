# REBT-Style Coaching: Tools & Principles

## Scope note

Rational Emotive Behaviour Therapy was developed by Albert Ellis in the 1950s and is a clinical therapeutic modality. A "REBT coach" is not an REBT therapist. The tools below help with everyday irrational thinking — rigid demands, catastrophising, low frustration tolerance, self-downing. They are not a substitute for therapy when therapy is needed.

## The core insight

*People are not disturbed by things, but by the view they take of things.* — Epictetus (which Ellis quoted often)

It is not the event that produces your suffering. It is what you believe about the event. Change the belief and the suffering changes, even when the event doesn't.

## The ABC model

The foundational tool. Three letters:

- **A — Activating event.** Something happens. Your boss criticises your work. Your friend doesn't call back. Traffic is bad.
- **B — Belief.** You interpret the event. Usually silently, automatically, and often irrationally.
- **C — Consequence.** You feel something and do something. The feeling and behaviour flow from the belief, not the event.

Most people think A causes C directly. Ellis's point: B does most of the work. Same A, different B, different C.

Add **D — Dispute** (challenge the irrational belief) and **E — Effective new belief**, and you have the full ABC(DE) model.

## The four core irrational beliefs

Ellis categorised most irrational thinking into four patterns:

### 1. Demandingness ("musturbation")

Rigid, absolutist demands on self, others, or the world:

- "I **must** succeed at this."
- "You **must** treat me fairly."
- "Life **must** be easy and comfortable."

The world doesn't have to comply with your demands. When it doesn't, demandingness produces disproportionate suffering.

The fix: convert demands to strong preferences.

- "I **strongly want** to succeed at this — and it would be very bad if I didn't, but not unbearable."
- "I **would strongly prefer** you to treat me fairly — but you're free to do what you do, and I'll respond accordingly."

### 2. Awfulising

Treating bad events as 100% catastrophic when they are, in fact, bad but not catastrophic.

- "It would be **awful** if I got rejected."
- "It would be the **end of the world** if I failed this exam."
- "This is the **worst thing** that could happen."

Almost none of this is true. Awful is a word that should be reserved for events that permanently end life or crush all future possibility. Most bad things are bad, painful, disappointing, hard — not awful.

The fix: proportionate language. "It would be bad." "It would be painful." "I would be very disappointed." Full stop.

### 3. Low frustration tolerance ("can't-stand-it-itis")

"I **can't stand** it when people are late."
"I **can't bear** being criticised."

Almost always, you can stand it. You are standing it right now. The belief that you can't makes frustration much larger than it needs to be.

The fix: "I don't like it. I would prefer it didn't happen. But I can stand it. I am standing it right now." That stance raises frustration tolerance without suppressing the legitimate dislike.

### 4. Global rating of self and others

"I'm a **failure**." (Not "I failed at this one thing.")
"He's a **terrible person**." (Not "He behaved badly.")
"I'm **worthless**."

The move is from behaviour to identity. "I did a bad thing" becomes "I am a bad person." This is a category error. Humans are too complex and variable to be summarised as worthwhile or worthless.

The fix: rate behaviours, not beings. "I behaved badly in this instance and I'm going to behave better next time." "He acted like a jerk today." Refuse the global rating.

## Healthy vs unhealthy negative emotions

REBT distinguishes between proportionate and disproportionate painful emotions:

| Healthy | Unhealthy |
|---|---|
| Sadness | Depression |
| Concern | Anxiety |
| Annoyance | Rage |
| Regret | Guilt (crushing) |
| Disappointment | Despair |
| Sorrow | Shame |

Both are unpleasant. The healthy ones flow from rational beliefs about real losses; the unhealthy ones flow from irrational beliefs about the same events. REBT targets the second, not the first.

The goal is not to stop feeling bad when bad things happen. It is to feel proportionately, and recover proportionately.

## Disputation — the three questions

Once you've identified an irrational belief, dispute it on three grounds:

1. **Empirical dispute:** Where is the evidence that this is true? What would hold up in court?
2. **Logical dispute:** Does this follow logically? Because I want X, does it really follow that I **must** have X?
3. **Pragmatic dispute:** Does believing this help me or hurt me? What does holding this belief cost me? What would a more rational belief produce?

All three together are more powerful than any one. Together they give the irrational belief nowhere to hide.

## Unconditional self-acceptance (USA)

The big move. Refuse to rate your overall worth as a human.

You are not a good person or a bad person. You are a fallible human who does many things — some well, some badly, most in between. Your worth is not up for negotiation based on performance, approval, or circumstances. Drop the whole project of rating yourself globally.

This is distinct from self-esteem. Self-esteem rises and falls with success and failure — which is inherently unstable. Unconditional self-acceptance is a stable stance regardless of outcome. You act, you succeed or fail, you notice what worked and what didn't, and your worth is not in play.

Ellis's companion moves: **unconditional other-acceptance** (refuse to globally rate other people too), and **unconditional life-acceptance** (refuse to rate life as a whole as good or bad).

## Vigorous disputation

Ellis was known for disputing irrational beliefs with force, even theatricality. The tools are weaker when used gently because irrational beliefs are themselves held with force. Mild reasoning bounces off them.

Practise disputing aloud. In writing. Multiple times. Find the strongest evidence and the sharpest logical contradiction. Mock the belief when that fits. The belief should feel genuinely challenged, not politely considered.

## Acting rationally in the world

Believing rationally is half the work. The other half is *acting* on the rational belief — especially in situations where the old irrational belief would have driven avoidance.

- If you've disputed "I must be approved of," act in a way that risks disapproval this week.
- If you've disputed "I can't stand frustration," sit with a frustration without distracting yourself.
- If you've disputed global self-rating, behave imperfectly on purpose and see what survives.

The new belief becomes durable when lived, not just thought.

## A pocket summary

- Your beliefs cause most of your suffering, not the events.
- Demands, awfulising, can't-stand-it-itis, and global ratings are the main culprits.
- Dispute them empirically, logically, and pragmatically.
- Replace with strong preferences, proportionate evaluation, and rating of behaviour not being.
- Accept yourself, others, and life unconditionally — as a stance, not a feeling.
- Then go act like a rational person in the world.
