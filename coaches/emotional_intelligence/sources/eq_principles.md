# Emotional Intelligence Principles

## The four domains

EQ is usually broken into four skills, roughly in this order:

1. **Self-awareness** — knowing what you're feeling, in real time, with some precision.
2. **Self-regulation** — choosing your response rather than being driven by the emotion.
3. **Empathy** — accurately sensing what others feel, with appropriate care.
4. **Social skill** — navigating conversations, conflicts, and relationships well.

Each depends on the one before it. You cannot regulate what you cannot name. You cannot empathise if you cannot first notice your own state. You cannot have social skill without the other three.

## Name it to tame it

A foundational finding from affective neuroscience (Lieberman and others): labelling an emotion reduces its physiological intensity. "I'm feeling a mix of disappointed and ashamed" is more regulating than "I feel bad."

Precision matters. Upgrade your vocabulary.

- Instead of **stressed** — try anxious, overloaded, pressured, wired, frazzled.
- Instead of **angry** — try frustrated, resentful, indignant, irritated, furious, hurt (often anger is hurt with armor on).
- Instead of **sad** — try disappointed, grieving, tender, deflated, hollow, wistful.
- Instead of **fine** — try content, steady, tired-but-okay, neutral, a little flat.

The more precisely you can name a feeling, the less it runs you.

## Emotions live in the body

Every emotion has a body signature: anger is usually heat, tightness, forward-leaning. Fear is usually cold, shallow breathing, pulled-inward. Grief is usually heavy, in the chest or throat.

Before you analyse an emotion, locate it. Where in the body? What sensation? What wants to happen — fight, flee, freeze, reach out?

This isn't woo. It is how emotions are actually encoded. Ignoring the body and thinking your way out of a feeling is like ignoring the check-engine light and debating the dashboard.

## Reaction vs response

- **Reaction** is automatic, fast, and shaped by old patterns. It runs before you choose.
- **Response** is chosen, a beat slower, shaped by your values.

The gap between stimulus and response is where freedom lives. EQ work is largely the work of widening that gap.

Practices that widen it:
- Breath: one long exhale before speaking.
- Naming: "I notice I'm getting defensive."
- Delay: "Let me get back to you on that in an hour."
- Body: stand up, walk to the window, change physiology before deciding.

## Feelings are data, not instructions

A feeling tells you something matters. It does not tell you what to do about it. Anger points to a boundary violation — but not every anger requires confrontation. Sadness points to a loss — but not every sadness requires action today.

Listen to the feeling. Decide the action separately. Most emotional regret comes from treating the feeling as the instruction.

## The trigger beneath the trigger

Surface trigger: she didn't text back.
Underlying trigger: reminds you of feeling unimportant in your family growing up.

Most disproportionate reactions are the current event touching a much older wound. When your reaction feels bigger than the trigger, ask: *what else does this remind me of?* The answer usually explains the size of the feeling.

This doesn't make the current event fine. It just helps you respond to the current event, not the old one.

## Empathy, not enmeshment

Empathy is sensing what another person feels and caring. It is not taking on their feelings as your own, fixing their feelings for them, or abandoning yourself to make them comfortable.

- **Healthy empathy:** "I can see you're hurt. I care. What do you need?"
- **Enmeshment:** "If you're upset, I'm upset. I can't be okay until you're okay."

The first is a gift. The second is a burden that eventually turns into resentment. Practise holding their feeling alongside your own steadiness.

## Perspective-taking is a rehearsable skill

Before concluding someone acted badly, run the exercise: *what would have to be true for their behaviour to make sense from the inside?* What pressure are they under that I don't know about? What is their fear? What story are they telling themselves?

This is not to excuse them. It is to understand. Often the behaviour is still wrong, but the understanding changes how you respond.

## The hardest conversation, handled well

A difficult conversation held with EQ usually has this shape:

1. **Set up:** "There's something I want to talk about. I care about this relationship. I might not say it perfectly."
2. **Observation (not interpretation):** "When X happened..." (fact, not story)
3. **Impact:** "I felt Y." (your experience, owned)
4. **Need:** "What I want is Z." (positive, specific)
5. **Invitation:** "What's your experience of this?" (their turn, really listening)

Not a formula to robot through. A shape to lean on when the feelings are strong and the stakes matter.

## Emotional granularity predicts wellbeing

People who can distinguish many subtly different emotions cope better with stress, drink less, have better relationships, and report higher wellbeing (research by Barrett and others). The vocabulary is not cosmetic — it changes what your brain can do with what you feel.

Worth the investment.
