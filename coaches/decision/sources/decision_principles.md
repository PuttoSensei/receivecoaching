# Decision-Making Principles

## Separate the decision from the outcome

A good decision can have a bad outcome (you made a reasonable bet; luck went the other way). A bad decision can have a good outcome (you were careless; you got lucky). Judging decisions only by outcomes is called resulting — and it produces worse decisions over time.

Evaluate a decision on its quality given what you knew AT THE TIME. Not on how it turned out. This is Annie Duke's foundational point and it changes how you think about your own choices.

## Match rigour to stakes

Most people do this backwards. They agonise over small reversible decisions (which restaurant, which app, which colour) and race through big irreversible ones (whether to take the job, whether to move in together, whether to have children).

- **Reversible, low-stakes:** decide fast. Five minutes. Pick good enough. Move on.
- **Reversible, high-stakes:** a few hours or days. Gather information, decide, iterate.
- **Irreversible, low-stakes:** a few days. Consider carefully once.
- **Irreversible, high-stakes:** weeks or months. Professional advice, quiet reflection, multiple angles, sometimes sleep on it.

Check the type before you begin. Don't treat a dinner choice like a marriage.

## One-way vs two-way doors

Jeff Bezos's frame, useful widely:

- **One-way door:** the decision is hard to reverse. Once through, you live with it. Firing someone. Publishing a book. Shutting down a product. Major surgery. These deserve time.
- **Two-way door:** the decision is easily reversed. You can walk back. Trying a diet. Launching an experiment. Most product decisions. Most hiring (you can adjust after). These deserve speed, not caution.

Most one-way doors are treated like two-way (rushed). Most two-way doors are treated like one-way (agonised over). Reversing this pattern makes you a better decider.

## The WRAP framework (Heath brothers)

For medium-to-big decisions:

- **W**iden your options.
  Most decisions start as binary: "should I or shouldn't I". Usually there are more options. Can you do a smaller version? A later version? Both at once? Neither, doing something different? Ask: "what else?" until you have at least three real options.

- **R**eality-test your assumptions.
  What are you assuming will happen? How would you know if you're wrong? Talk to people who've done this. Run a small experiment before the big commitment. Look for evidence that contradicts, not just confirms.

- **A**ttain distance before deciding.
  Decisions made hot are often poor. Get perspective:
  - "What would I tell a friend facing this?"
  - "What would I think about this decision at 80 looking back?"
  - "If I left the company, what would my successor do?"
  Distance reveals what proximity hides.

- **P**repare to be wrong.
  What will you do if this doesn't work? Pre-decide. This both reduces the scariness of committing and makes course-correction faster if it turns out to be wrong.

## 10/10/10

Suzy Welch's time horizon tool:

- How will I feel about this decision in 10 minutes?
- How about in 10 months?
- How about in 10 years?

The three time horizons often give different answers. Usually the 10-years horizon is the one that matters most. 10 minutes is often fear or craving; 10 years is often values.

## Inversion

Instead of asking "how do I make this succeed?", ask "what would guarantee failure?" Then avoid those things.

This often produces clearer action than direct optimisation. It's easier to know what kills a project than what guarantees success. Avoid the killers.

Charlie Munger: "All I want to know is where I'm going to die so I'll never go there."

## Regret minimisation

For big life decisions, imagine yourself at 80, looking back. Which option will you regret less?

This cuts through short-term noise — the fear of what people will think, the risk of looking foolish, the comfort of the familiar. The older-you perspective consistently weights differently than the current-you perspective.

Bezos used this to decide whether to leave a good hedge fund job to start Amazon. It applies to much smaller decisions too.

## Satisficing beats maximising

Herbert Simon's insight. For most decisions, you don't need the optimal answer. You need a good-enough answer.

Maximising everything is exhausting and produces diminishing returns. Pick a standard (good enough looks like ___), find the first option that meets it, commit, move on.

For most decisions, the difference between "best" and "good enough" is marginal. The cost of continuing to optimise is significant.

Maximise the decisions that matter most. Satisfice everything else.

## Sunk cost is gone

What you've already invested in something — time, money, effort, years — is gone either way. Whether you continue or stop, you don't get it back.

The question is never "how much have I invested?" It's always "from here forward, given what I now know, is this the right move?"

This applies to: jobs you've been in for years, relationships you've invested in, projects you've committed to, money you've spent. Sunk cost biases people toward continuation when quitting would be better.

## The 70% rule

If you wait for 90% certainty, you're too slow. Decide at about 70% confidence and course-correct as new information arrives.

Speed matters, especially for reversible decisions. Waiting for certainty is often procrastination in disguise.

The exception: one-way doors deserve more certainty. But most decisions aren't those.

## Common traps

- **Confirmation bias.** You gather evidence for what you've already decided and call it research. Actively seek disconfirming evidence.
- **Availability bias.** What comes to mind first feels more important. Recent or vivid examples dominate reasoning.
- **Recency bias.** Over-weighting the latest data point.
- **Loss aversion.** Fear of losing what you have often outweighs rational assessment of what you'd gain.
- **Decision fatigue.** Late in the day, in the middle of a hard week, decisions are worse. Don't make big ones when depleted.
- **Emotional contagion.** Other people's anxiety (or excitement) pulls your decision in that direction. Get distance from them before deciding.
- **The option you haven't considered.** The right answer is often outside the binary you're stuck in.
- **Deciding for the wrong self.** Deciding to please past-you, imagined-future-you, parents, peers — rather than actual-present-you.

## Emotion as data

Fear is information. Excitement is information. Dread is information. None of them are commands.

A decision made purely from fear usually underperforms. A decision that completely ignores fear (or dread or excitement) usually misses important signal.

Integration: notice the emotion. Ask what it's telling you. Then decide from a more integrated place. "I'm scared AND this still looks like the right move" is different from both "I'm scared so no" and "I'm scared but I'm ignoring it."

## The decision journal

For important decisions, write it down at the time:
- What is the decision?
- What are my options?
- What do I expect to happen?
- What's my confidence level?
- What am I uncertain about?
- How will I know if it's working?

Then, 3–6 months later, reread. This is how you learn your own decision-making patterns. Most people never do this and keep making the same type of mistake.

## When stuck

If you've been analysing for weeks and can't decide, the analysis is not the bottleneck. Something else is:

- Fear of committing
- Hoping the decision will become clear without you having to choose
- Avoiding the conversation with someone the decision affects
- Unresolved underlying question (do I want this at all?)

Push through with: "Which option would I be glad I chose at the end of next year?" Or: "I'm going to decide by Friday, whichever way." Deadlines force decisions.

## Commit after deciding

After you decide, commit. Don't relitigate every week. Don't keep the other options alive in your head. Act as if this is the right call and adjust only if new evidence arrives.

People who second-guess decisions forever get less of their life's benefit from any of them. The decision was the point. The execution is now.

If new information arrives that genuinely changes things — fine, course-correct openly. If you're just anxious, sit with the anxiety, don't undo the decision.

## A decision practice

For any non-trivial decision:
1. What type is this? (Reversibility, stakes.)
2. What are my options — beyond the first binary?
3. What am I assuming? What would change my mind?
4. How will this look in 10 months? 10 years?
5. What's the cost of waiting vs deciding now?
6. Set a deadline. Decide. Commit.

This takes 15 minutes for small decisions, a few hours for medium ones, and is woven across days for big ones. It beats not deciding.
