# Wellness Principles

## The hierarchy

Wellness is not one thing. It is a stack of interacting systems. Most people try to optimise the top and ignore the bottom. This always fails. Work upstream first:

1. **Sleep** — 7–9 hours, consistent timing
2. **Daylight and outdoor time** — especially morning
3. **Movement** — any, daily
4. **Food basics** — whole food, protein, fibre, water
5. **Stress management and recovery** — boundaries, rest, solitude
6. **Connection** — quality relationships, non-transactional contact
7. **Then:** supplements, biohacks, advanced practices, optimisation

No meditation app fixes sleep deprivation. No $300 supplement beats a walk outside. No productivity hack helps a person who has said yes to too many things.

## Sleep is the foundation

If you are not sleeping well, nothing downstream works properly. Food decisions go bad. Mood goes low. Willpower evaporates. Workouts underperform. Relationships fray.

The basics of sleep hygiene:
- Consistent bed and wake times, even weekends.
- Dark room, cool temperature, quiet.
- No screens 30–60 minutes before bed — or at minimum, dimmed and warmed.
- Limit caffeine past early afternoon.
- Limit alcohol — it fragments sleep even when it helps you fall asleep.
- Outside within the first hour of waking, for morning light exposure.
- A wind-down routine you actually keep.

If sleep is consistently disrupted for weeks despite these, see a doctor. Sleep apnea, anxiety, hormonal changes, and other conditions are common and treatable.

## Movement

You do not need a gym, a programme, or expensive gear. You need to move daily, with a mix of:

- **Walking** — 7–10k steps most days. The most underrated wellness intervention.
- **Strength** — 2–3 times a week. Bodyweight is fine to start. Resistance training is a core health lever that most people neglect.
- **Cardio** — get breathless a few times a week. Does not need to be running.
- **Mobility** — some stretching, yoga, or range-of-motion work several times a week.
- **Play** — games, dancing, sports, roughhousing with kids or dogs. Movement that isn't a task.

The single biggest predictor of whether someone keeps moving over decades is whether they enjoy it. Find what you like.

## Daylight

Thirty minutes of outdoor light most days, ideally in the first hour of waking, regulates circadian rhythm, supports sleep, supports mood, and supports vitamin D status.

Most indoor-workers are catastrophically under-lit. This affects more than people realise.

## Stress is not the enemy

Stress is unavoidable and in moderate doses actually useful. Chronic stress without recovery is the enemy.

The simple question: *am I recovering between stressors?* Not "am I stressed" but "am I rebounding".

Recovery looks like: sleep, rest, nature, connection, movement that is joyful rather than punishing, solitude, creative time, doing nothing for a little while without feeling guilty.

If you cannot remember the last time you rested fully, that is the intervention.

## Phone and screen hygiene

Wellness is downstream of your attention environment. A phone that interrupts all day is an unwellness machine.

Simple moves:
- No phone in the bedroom.
- No phone first thing in the morning — give the first hour to yourself.
- Notifications off for everything that doesn't matter (almost everything).
- One app blocker if needed — friction is your friend.
- Screen-free meals, at least sometimes.

Nothing hurts my clients' wellness more reliably than unmanaged phone use. It eats sleep, attention, mood, and connection.

## Connection as a wellness pillar

The Harvard Study of Adult Development (80+ years, longest study of its kind) found that the biggest predictor of lifelong health and happiness was the quality of close relationships. Not income. Not fame. Not achievement. Relationships.

This means: wellness includes tending to the people you love. Scheduled calls. Shared meals. Being known and knowing others. Friendships maintained over years. Loneliness is as hard on the body as smoking a packet a day.

Ask: is there anyone I haven't seen in too long? Do that before biohacking.

## Avoid optimisation porn

A lot of contemporary wellness content is for people with $500/week budgets and no children. Cold plunges, infrared saunas, peptides, stacks of supplements, 5am routines.

This is not wellness. It is wellness as status performance. Most of it is unnecessary, and almost none of it beats fixing sleep, walking daily, eating whole food, and having real friendships.

Be suspicious of anything that costs a lot, sounds new, and has weak evidence. Do the boring things. The boring things work.

## A weekly wellness baseline

- **Sleep:** 7 nights of 7+ hours, consistent timing.
- **Movement:** 5+ days of walking, 2+ days of strength, occasional harder effort.
- **Outside:** daily, even briefly.
- **Food:** whole food most of the time, protein at meals, water first.
- **Rest:** one full evening that is nothing.
- **Connection:** one real conversation with someone who matters.
- **Screens:** less than you do now.
- **No phone:** first hour of morning, last hour of night.

Do most of this most weeks and you will be in the top decile of your peer group for health and wellbeing, without any exotic practices.

## When to escalate

If despite the basics you have persistent fatigue, pain, mood disturbance, digestive issues, or other symptoms for more than a few weeks, see a doctor. Wellness coaching is not diagnosis. Real symptoms deserve real investigation.
